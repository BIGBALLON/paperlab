GLM-5 · DSA on an MLA Base, and Sequential RL That Doesn't Forget

I spent two evenings with the GLM-5 technical report (arXiv:2602.15763v2, 2026-02-24) and the public HF config, and came away thinking the abstract sells the scale while the body sells the engineering. The 355B→744B parameter jump is the headline, sure. But the three things that make this paper worth reading twice are subtler. First: DSA was retrofitted onto an MLA base in 20B tokens, versus DeepSeek-V3.2's 943.7B — a 1/47 budget for nearly the same long-context quality. Second: Muon Split, a one-line tweak to how Muon orthogonalizes MLA projection matrices, closes the MLA-vs-GQA quality gap without any logit-clipping hack. Third: the post-training is a sequential Reasoning→Agentic→General RL pipeline glued together by on-policy cross-stage distillation — the anti-catastrophic-forgetting mechanism that lets each stage earn its benchmark without washing out the last one. What follows is a geek's reading log: the parts I found load-bearing, the ablations I re-ran in my head, and the exactly-one place the paper prose drifts from the shipped config.

TL;DR

GLM-5 is what you get when you take the GLM-4.5 MoE recipe, double the expert count, double the total params, and then pay two specific costs honestly. Architectural cost: MLA under the Muon optimizer loses to GQA-8 at the same compute budget (Paper Table 1 — 33.5 vs 38.5 on HumanEval) — the fix, Muon Split, orthogonalizes per head rather than per projection matrix, so head-specific scales float independently and logit blow-up stops happening structurally. Long-context cost: training DSA from scratch like DeepSeek-V3.2 would burn 943.7B tokens — so GLM-5 trains MLA the whole way, then warms up a 32-head 128-dim lightning indexer on frozen weights for 1k steps and jointly trains backbone+indexer for just 20B tokens, recovering within 0.35 points of MLA at 128K. Post-training cost: three sequential RL stages (Reasoning→Agentic→General) should catastrophically forget; they don't, because every stage distills on-policy from the previous-stage checkpoint — reasoning-edge gets re-encoded through the agent-RL weights rather than overwritten. Net: the paper is mostly about how you don't pay cost you should have paid.

overview // what 5 actually changes over 4.5

Quick family tree, because these ship fast. GLM-4.5 was a 355B/32B-active MoE (160 routed experts, 8 per token, 1 shared, GQA-8 attention, Muon optimizer), pre-trained on 23T tokens. GLM-4.7 was an incremental release; it doesn't have its own paper but its numbers appear as the previous-generation column in most of GLM-5's tables. GLM-5 is this paper, 2026-02-17 first drop, 2026-02-24 v2; and GLM-5.1 is the long-horizon-optimization follow-up dated 2026-04-07. For this read, the axis that matters is 4.5→5, which the paper §1 summarizes as four technical contributions. I'll paraphrase how they actually compose:

  1. Scale up behind DSA. 744B total, 40B active, 256 routed experts of which 8 activate per token (same shared-expert-of-1 pattern as 4.5), 28.5T tokens total training budget. DSA (DeepSeek Sparse Attention) keeps attention cost sub-quadratic at 128K+. Plain scaling — but with DSA, not with more GQA layers.
  2. Async RL infrastructure. Building on the slime framework from GLM-4.5, the new infrastructure decouples rollout generation from training updates, with a stateful DP-aware routing layer that pins an entire agent rollout's sequential requests to one DP rank so the MoE KV cache stays locally hot.
  3. Asynchronous Agent RL algorithms. The previous generation trained agents with iterative self-distillation and outcome supervision. GLM-5 adds a GRPO+IcePop variant (KL term deleted) plus trajectory filtering that drops samples too far off-policy and samples that failed due to sandbox crashes rather than model error.
  4. Full-stack Chinese NPU adaptation. Seven platforms — Ascend, Moore Threads, Hygon, Cambricon, Kunlunxin, MetaX, Enflame — with a W4A8 quantization recipe on Ascend, a Lightning Indexer kernel, and an MLAPO “super operator” that fuses 13 pre-processing ops. Real engineering work; not the paper's load-bearing idea and I won't dwell on it.

If I had to collapse the four into three load-bearing bets that the rest of the article elaborates, they'd be: (A) MLA + Muon Split as the stability recipe that makes MLA competitive under Muon without a QK-norm or softcap; (B) DSA via continued pre-training from an MLA base as the efficient path to lossless sparse attention; and (C) Sequential RL with on-policy cross-stage distillation as the post-training structure. Each of the three has a clear counterfactual: if A failed you'd be clipping logits (like Kimi K2's MuonClip), if B failed you'd be spending an order of magnitude more tokens like DeepSeek-V3.2 did, if C failed your agent-RL model would quietly lose points on math benchmarks. The paper's contribution is that none of those worst-cases materialized.

architecture // what's inside the 744B

At its skeleton, GLM-5 is a DeepSeek-V3-family MoE: a stack of transformer layers where a few early layers are dense and the rest route to a shared expert plus 8-of-256 routed experts. What's specific to GLM-5 is three things: the attention block, which is MLA with a compute-rebalanced head dimension (MLA-256) wrapped in DSA sparsity; the MTP block, which is a single-layer shared-parameter MTP that predicts 3 tokens during training; and the routing, which keeps the GLM-4.5 / DeepSeek-V3 noaux_tc no-auxiliary-loss scheme with scoring_func = sigmoid. Notice the MoE+DSA column is 75 layers wide against 3 dense layers on the left — the vast majority of the forward pass goes through DSA-sparsified MLA. The dense-first-three pattern is inherited from GLM-4.5 and DeepSeek-V3; the novelty here is the full-width DSA column and the parameter-shared MTP on the right.

Three things about this figure are worth naming out loud because they're the ones people will argue about on HackerNews. First, the shipped config is glm_moe_dsa with num_hidden_layers=78 and num_nextn_predict_layers=1; the 78 layers decompose as 3 dense + 75 MoE-with-DSA. The paper's §2.1 prose summary says “reduces its layer count to 80,” which is a one-off-by-one rounded-up statement; the companion Table 10 in the same paper lists 3 dense + 75 MoE + 1 MTP = 79. The config and table agree; the prose drifts by one. I'll file this properly in the drift section below.

Second, the attention head dimensions are unusual and worth reading twice. The config has qk_head_dim=256, qk_nope_head_dim=192, and qk_rope_head_dim=64; Paper Table 10 reports QK Head Dim 192. Both are right — the per-head query/key has a content (non-positional) component of 192 dims and a RoPE component of 64 dims, summed to 256. The paper is reporting the nope part; the config is reporting the total. It's a convention difference, not drift. The reason the total was bumped from DeepSeek-V3's 192 to GLM-5's 256 is explicit in §2.1: “While the number of attention heads in DeepSeek-V3 is selected according to the roofline of H800, it is inappropriate for other hardware. Given the Multi-head Attention (MHA) style of MLA during training and prefilling, we increase the head dimension from 192 to 256 and decrease the number of attention heads by 1/3. This keeps the training computation and the number of parameters constant while decreasing the decoding computation.” That paragraph buries something I think is important — we'll get to it in the next section.

Third, rope_theta = 1,000,000. The paper doesn't state this number anywhere I could find, but it's what the shipped model runs with. For comparison: DeepSeek-V3 uses 10,000, Kimi K2 raised it to 50,000, Qwen3-VL uses 5,000,000. So GLM-5 sits in the Llama-3 / mid-range territory, not the extended-context high values. That's consistent with the 200K training window — you don't need 5e6 rope_theta to serve a 200K context; 1e6 is plenty.

MoE routing mechanics: sigmoid + noaux_tc + renorm

GLM-5 uses the no-aux-loss (noaux_tc) router from DeepSeek-V3 with a sigmoid scoring function. It's five operations stacked in the right order; the order matters more than any single term. Inline from the shipped config.json:

Notice the flow from sigmoid to top-K to renormalize: the sigmoid produces independent probabilities, the bias shifts them to rebalance load, the top-K selects on the biased value, but the final weights renormalize only the raw sigmoid probabilities — so a heavily-biased expert gets picked more often but contributes only what its real score says it should.

Why noaux_tc instead of an auxiliary loss. The classical MoE load-balance loss (e.g. Switch Transformer's) adds a term to the training objective that penalizes imbalance. Every language-modeling gradient step is then fighting two directions at once — predict tokens well, and make the routing uniform. GLM-5's topk_method = "noaux_tc" replaces that term with a bias b_e per expert, updated online based on recent routing load, and kept out of the loss entirely. The routing gradient no longer competes with the language gradient; the bias takes care of balance at the bookkeeping level. If you remove the bias and put back the aux loss, you reintroduce the gradient tug-of-war; if you remove the bias without adding anything back, the hot experts starve the cold ones and you get the "one expert does all the work" failure mode that killed early sparse-MoE scaling.

Why 8 of 256, not 8 of 128 or 16 of 256

GLM-4.5 routed 8 of 160 experts (5% activation); GLM-5 routes 8 of 256 (3.1%). Same active-count, wider routing pool. The active-parameter budget per token didn't change much (32B → 40B, a 25% bump, not 60%); what changed is the specialization surface. More experts at the same active-K means finer-grained specialization — each expert carries less capacity and covers a narrower sub-task. DeepSeek-V3 showed this empirically at 8-of-256, Qwen3-next pushed it further, and GLM-5 is inheriting the consensus.

The alternative designs the paper didn't ship each have a specific failure mode. 8 of 128: fewer experts, so each expert has larger moe_intermediate_size and routing pressure is high; the noaux_tc bias works but some experts become bottleneck-hot. 16 of 256: doubles active params per token, so active → 80B which blows the serving budget on a single Ascend node; also reduces the effective specialization pressure because any given expert is more likely to fire. 16 of 512: the limit — expert count dominates all-to-all communication latency. The chosen 8-of-256 plus 1-shared-expert geometry lands on the Pareto front the DeepSeek-V3 family established: routing sparsity high enough that specialization emerges, active count low enough for single-node serving, shared expert catches what the router misroutes.

The shared expert is not decorative. With 1 shared expert always active plus 8 of 256 routed, the model can put "generally useful" computation in the shared expert (token-level preprocessing, syntactic patterns, high-frequency features) and reserve the routed experts for specialization. Without a shared expert, every routed-expert has to waste some capacity on the always-needed work, reducing effective specialization. DeepSeek-V3 ablates this; GLM-5 inherits the shared-expert decision without re-ablating, which is the correct research-economy call — don't re-run ablations someone else already ran if you're inheriting the result.

MTP as one physical layer, three logical predictions

num_nextn_predict_layers=1 in the config is misleading at first glance. During training, the paper reuses that one physical layer three times to predict the next three tokens. Each reuse has its own set of input/output projections but shares the transformer block — so memory footprint stays at "1 MTP layer", while the supervision signal covers three future positions.

Notice the three arrows from the MTP block to k=1/2/3 — same block, three passes, three CE losses summed at training. At inference the block runs once with k=1 by default (speculative decoding proposes 3 tokens; the main LM head verifies). That's how accept length 2.76 is compatible with "one MTP layer in the weights file".

Why 2.76 beats DeepSeek-V3.2's 2.55 given the same 4-step budget. DeepSeek-V3 trains one MTP layer that predicts only the next 2 tokens; at inference, positions beyond k=2 are out-of-distribution for that single layer, so the acceptance rate on the 3rd and 4th speculative tokens degrades. GLM-5's parameter-shared variant trains the one physical layer three times — at offsets k=1, 2, 3 — during every backward pass. Each offset sees the same layer weights during training, so the layer's next-token distribution is jointly calibrated for all three horizons. That's why accept length goes up: the 3rd speculative token is no longer distribution-shifted; it was a training objective. And the memory cost is still one MTP layer's worth of parameters, because the physical block is the same tensor shared across passes.

the three-layer recipe you don't want

The obvious alternative — three separate MTP layers, each specialized to one offset — doubles-plus memory and forces you to route KV cache across three draft heads at serve. Paper §2.1: "the memory usage of MTP parameters and the kv cache scales linearly with the number of speculative steps." Parameter sharing dodges both costs: one set of weights, one KV path, three training signals. The cost is that the shared layer has to be good at all three horizons simultaneously — which is fine empirically, because "predict the next k-th token given k-1 previously-predicted tokens" is a coherent family of objectives, not three conflicting ones.

This is the section of the paper I underlined the most. The setup: GLM-4.5 trained with Muon on GQA-8 attention and the logits stayed stable. When the GLM-5 team tried to move to MLA (576-dim latent KV, standard DeepSeek-V2-style shape) under the same Muon recipe, the model underperformed — per Paper Table 1 on base-model downstream evals:

What Muon Split actually is, in one equation

Before the equation, a picture of the block. MLA in GLM-5 is a two-step projection: low-rank down-project the hidden state through a LoRA-style bottleneck, then up-project into per-head query / key / value. The Muon-unfriendly step is the up-projection, because that's the matrix that stacks all heads in a single [d_head · n_heads, d_latent] shape. Here's the block:

Notice the pink W_UQ / W_UK / W_UV columns — those are the Muon Split targets. The orthogonalization used to run on each pink block as a single matrix (joint singular spectrum across 64 heads); Muon Split runs it on each per-head sub-block independently. Same optimizer, different invariance. The green panel at the bottom shows why it works; the orange panel shows what used to break.

Muon, used here as the pre-training optimizer, applies orthogonalization (a Newton-Schulz approximation of the zeroth power) to the update. The GLM-4.5 recipe applied this orthogonalization to whole multi-head projection matrices — call them $W^{UQ}, W^{UK}, W^{UV} \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{head}} n_{\text{heads}} \times d_{\text{latent}}}$. For GQA that was fine; for MLA it wasn't. The fix is one line:

$$ W^{UQ} \;=\; \left[ W^{UQ}_1, W^{UQ}_2, \ldots, W^{UQ}_{n_{\text{heads}}}\right] \quad\text{and apply Muon orthogonalization to each }W^{UQ}_h\text{ independently}. $$

And the per-head Newton-Schulz step that Muon Split now runs independently per block:

$$ X_{k+1} \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\left(3 X_k - X_k X_k^{\top} X_k\right),\qquad X_0 = W^{UQ}_h / \|W^{UQ}_h\|_F,\; k = 0, 1, 2 $$

Three iterations is what Muon typically runs; the output approximates the orthogonal factor of the polar decomposition of $W^{UQ}_h$ — "the zeroth power", as the Muon authors like to say. The algebraic difference between Muon and Muon Split is exactly which matrix you feed into that iteration: the whole stacked per-head up-projection (joint spectrum), or each $W^{UQ}_h \in \mathbb{R}^{2048 \times 256}$ separately (64 independent spectra). Everything else in the optimizer is identical — same 0-momentum Muon schedule, same learning rate, same data order.

Instead of one big orthogonalization over the stacked per-head projections (which binds every head to the same scale after normalization), Muon Split splits the matrix by head before the Newton-Schulz step and orthogonalizes each head-block separately. Each head gets its own singular-value normalization. Paper §2.1 is explicit about the effect: “with Muon Split, the scale of attention logits of GLM-5 remains stable during pre-training without any clipping strategy.” That sentence is doing a lot of work — it's telling you that the whole MuonClip story (clip the per-head max logit at a threshold τ, then renormalize the weights) is being replaced by a training-time recipe that just never produces the pathological logits in the first place. The pain point MuonClip was invented for — Muon + MoE + large-scale pre-training explodes logits — still exists; GLM-5's answer is to constrain the optimizer rather than post-hoc-bound the activation.

why this is the right fix

Muon's Newton-Schulz orthogonalization normalizes the singular values of whatever matrix you hand it. When you hand it one big matrix that stacks 96 heads' worth of projection, you're asking it to normalize the joint singular spectrum — and the per-head subspaces end up sharing the normalization budget. Under MLA's compressed latent KV, some heads want much sharper attention than others, and a global normalization starves those heads of the scale they need; the weights compensate by growing, the logits grow, and eventually you need to clip. Muon Split lets each head have its own singular-value budget. Same optimizer, different invariance. My read: this is the sort of recipe tweak that's going to propagate to every Muon-trained MLA model released in 2026 because it makes the logit-clipping workaround unnecessary.

Why MLA-256 cuts head count by a third

The second subtlety is the MLA-256 variant. GLM-5 ships with qk_head_dim=256, num_attention_heads=64; a straight MLA (post-Muon-Split, row 3 of Table 1) had qk_head_dim=192, num_attention_heads=96. Why? The paper's justification (§2.1) is a hardware-roofline argument: at decode time, MLA dot-products over qk_head_dim + v_head_dim dims per head, and on non-H800 silicon the latency is head-count-dominated. So cut the head count.

The paper's phrasing is that the change “keeps the training computation and the number of parameters constant while decreasing the decoding computation.” Recompute it: attention matmul work scales as n_heads × head_dim, so vanilla-MLA is $96 \times 192 = 18{,}432$ and MLA-256 is $64 \times 256 = 16{,}384$. That's 11% less, not constant — the paper's “constant” rounds hard. The direction is still favorable (you're paying less in both training and decoding), so nothing breaks; but if you're sizing your cluster off the paper's exact claim, you'll over-provision by ~11% of the attention-block budget.

Where the decode wins show up is the number of dot products per token: $96 \to 64$ is a 33% reduction, and each of those dot products costs qk_head_dim + kv_lora_rank ≈ 256 + 512 = 768 element-wise MACs at decode. $96 \times 768 = 73{,}728$ vs $64 \times 768 = 49{,}152$, a 33% decode-path shrink per layer — which compounds across 78 layers. At 40B active params and 200K context, decode-path FLOPs are the bottleneck, not prefill. The MLA-256 row in Table 1 shows the benchmark cost of this rebalance is essentially nothing; that's the right trade for a model whose explicit positioning is agentic inference.

dsa via continued pre-training // 20B tokens, not 943.7B

Here's the contrast that structures this section. DeepSeek-V3.2 introduced DSA and adapted its dense V3.1 base into V3.2 over 943.7 billion tokens. GLM-5 adopts the same DSA primitive but starts from an MLA base — trained the whole way as MLA — and adapts it in 20 billion tokens. That's roughly 1/47 the budget. The paper's §2.1.1 claim is blunt: “Although the training budget is much smaller than that of DeepSeek-V3.2 (943.7B tokens), we find that it is enough to adapt the DSA model to match the performance of the original MLA model.” I want to unpack what makes that possible, because the answer isn't “DSA is easier” — it's “DSA from an MLA warmup is a much smaller adaptation than DSA from a dense warmup.”

What the indexer actually does, per query token

Notice the ReLU between steps 3 and 4 — without it a negative dot product on one head could cancel a positive match on another, and "sum of signed scores" becomes a noisy ranking signal. With ReLU, only positive matches contribute; the score is a soft count of "how many heads find this position relevant", which is a much more stable ranking signal for Top-2048.

The two-stage recipe, verbatim

01
Indexer warmup

1,000 steps, 14 sequences per step, each 202,752 tokens long. Max LR = 5e-3 (very high). Only the 32-head 128-dim lightning indexer is trained; all base model weights frozen. The indexer learns to score query-key similarity and produce the top-2048 set.

02
Sparse adaptation (20B tok)

Follows the data mixture and hyperparameters of mid-training. Both indexer and backbone trainable. Constant LR = 1e-5. Roughly 20 billion tokens total — the budget is set by how quickly the sparse-attention model matches the dense-MLA performance on RULER and on SFT losses.

03
Validation

Fine-tune both DSA and MLA bases with the same SFT data. Paper: “the two models tie in training loss and evaluation benchmarks.” Net: no quality tax from the DSA adaptation; all sparsity is free.

04
Serve & scale

Shipped model runs DSA on all 75 MoE layers; first 3 dense layers run plain MLA. Attention FLOPs at 128K+ drop by roughly 1.5–2× vs dense MLA. At 200K, the reduction is larger.

The detail I want to dwell on is the warmup step's learning rate: 5e-3. That's roughly 25× higher than GLM-5's pre-training max LR of 2e-4. The warmup is also short — 1,000 steps — and touches only the indexer weights. This is a targeted optimization: you're training a small module (32 heads × 128 dim, operating as a fast similarity scorer) on top of a frozen base, and you want it to converge in a thousand steps to “pick the important tokens,” so you crank the LR up and let it move. Once the indexer is good at ranking, stage 2 drops the LR to 1e-5 and jointly updates the backbone so it can start relying on the sparse set.

Why 20B is enough (a small ablation the paper runs in miniature)

Paper Table 6, which I think is underrated, runs the same DSA recipe on the much smaller GLM-4.7-Flash model — this is the sanity-check that makes the full-scale result believable. The recipe is identical: frozen-indexer warmup for 1k steps at batch 16, then 150B-token joint training. Here's RULER across context lengths:

Model 4K 8K 16K 32K 64K 128K
GLM-4.7-Flash (MLA base) 97.44 96.72 95.83 92.96 85.34 79.21

Read this table the way I did. Warmup alone recovers 90% of the RULER score across all context lengths under 64K; the gap at 128K is substantial (71.35 vs 79.21, an 8-point drop). After 150B tokens of joint training, the DSA model actually beats the MLA baseline at 16K, 32K, and 64K and loses by only 0.35 points at 128K. That's the principle landing: DSA is not a different model — it's the same model with a learned top-K selector over attention; given the selector and enough joint tokens, the sparse model's behavior converges to the dense model's. The reason GLM-5's main run uses only 20B tokens is that it's a 744B model, not a 4B-Flash-sized one; joint training at 744B is an order of magnitude more expensive per token, so the budget gets compressed.

The 744B evidence: Paper Table 3, the number most readers miss

The paper does publish a 744B MLA-vs-DSA comparison — it's Paper Table 3 in §2.1.1, one table before the big SWA/GDN ablation that gets all the attention. Four long-context retrieval benchmarks at 128K, run on the actual shipped 744B MLA base vs the DSA-adapted model after the 20B-token CPT:

Base (744B, 128K context) MQ-NIAH MV-NIAH SQuAD HotpotQA
MLA (dense attention) 100.0 95.5 79.7 66.3
DSA (20B-tok CPT, shipped) 100.0 97.0 86.0 63.0

DSA beats MLA on three of the four 128K retrieval benchmarks, loses 3.3 points on the fourth. Multi-query needle-in-a-haystack ties at ceiling; multi-value NIAH improves by 1.5; SQuAD-128K improves by 6.3. The only regression is HotpotQA's multi-hop reasoning. The point isn't that DSA is magic; the point is that content-addressed top-K sparsity actively filters retrieval distractors that dense attention has to wade through. SQuAD-128K at +6.3 is the clearest case: the task hides a short answer span in 128K of context, and the indexer's ReLU-sum-over-heads scoring surfaces exactly the passage that contains it. Dense MLA attends to everything and has to learn to down-weight the distractors via soft attention; DSA hard-selects the top 2048 positions and the distractors never enter the attention budget at all.

Why HotpotQA regresses when SQuAD improves. They measure different things. SQuAD-128K is single-hop — one passage contains the answer, distractors are noise; top-K selection is pure win. HotpotQA is multi-hop — the answer requires aggregating evidence from two or more passages, and the indexer might rank one of those passages outside the top 2048 if its surface similarity to the query is weaker. That's the failure mode of content-addressed sparsity: it's optimal for “find this”, suboptimal for “reason across.” The 3.3-point gap at 20B tokens is my best evidence that the adaptation budget is tight, not excessive — another 20B would likely close it, but the paper chose not to spend that.

Paper §2.1.2: “DSA is lossless by construction: its lightning indexer achieves token-level sparsity without discarding any long-range dependencies, enabling application to all layers with no quality degradation. … all of these methods incur an inherent accuracy gap on fine-grained retrieval tasks — up to 5.69 points on RULER@128K and 7.33 on RepoQA@128K — due to the unavoidable information loss introduced by efficient attention mechanisms during continual-training adaptation, even when half of the layers retain full attention.” I think the key phrase is “token-level sparsity”: DSA's indexer picks top-2048 tokens per query, which means the identities of the tokens retained vary with the query. SWA's window is fixed by position; GDN's state is learned but content-compressed. DSA is content-addressed sparsity, and that's why it has no quality floor. 

# Pseudocode for one DSA attention layer, based on paper §2.1.1 and the
# DeepSeek-V3.2 DSA formulation. The shipped config.json has these shapes.
# index_n_heads = 32, index_head_dim = 128, index_topk = 2048.

def dsa_attention(q, k, v, layer_idx):
    # MLA compressed KV is already cached: shape [B, L_ctx, kv_lora_rank=512]
    kv_cache = self.kv_cache[layer_idx]                         # L_ctx grows

    # 1. Lightning indexer: a cheap 32-head, 128-dim similarity scorer.
    #    Operates on the low-rank latent so it's O(32 * 128 * L_ctx).
    idx_q = self.index_q_proj(q)                                # [B, L_q, 32, 128]
    idx_k = self.index_k_proj(kv_cache)                         # [B, L_ctx, 32, 128]
    # RoPE on idx_q and idx_k (indexer_rope_interleave=True)
    idx_q, idx_k = apply_rope(idx_q, idx_k)
    scores = torch.einsum("blhd,bmhd->blhm", idx_q, idx_k)      # [B, L_q, 32, L_ctx]
    scores = F.relu(scores).sum(dim=2)                          # [B, L_q, L_ctx]

    # 2. Top-K: each query keeps only the 2048 most-similar tokens.
    #    L_ctx can be 200K; we dramatically shrink the attended set.
    topk_idx = scores.topk(k=2048, dim=-1).indices              # [B, L_q, 2048]

    # 3. Sparse MLA attention over just those 2048 tokens.
    #    q is projected to qk_head_dim=256 (192 nope + 64 rope), v to 256.
    #    num_attention_heads=64. This is where the real attn FLOPs go.
    return sparse_mla_attention(q, k, v, indices=topk_idx)

# The key move: ReLU-sum-over-index-heads in step 1 collapses the 32 heads to
# a single per-query scalar per candidate token. Top-K on that scalar picks
# the retained set. The 32 heads of the indexer are NOT the 64 heads of MLA
# itself — the indexer is a separate, cheap module whose only job is ranking.

The reason DSA is cheap to retrofit on an MLA base is that MLA's compressed latent KV is the exact input the indexer needs. The indexer doesn't need access to the full-dimensional keys; it operates on kv_lora_rank=512-dim latents, projected down further to 32 heads of 128 dims each. That's why a 1,000-step warmup — with frozen backbone weights — is enough to train a functional indexer. If you were doing DSA from a dense base (the DeepSeek-V3.2 path), you'd also be fighting an attention redesign, not just adding a selector. GLM-5 front-loaded the attention redesign into its pre-training and paid the DSA cost as pure selector training; that's the whole game, and it's the principle you can carry to the next model.

Mid-training: the stage that's really pre-training in disguise

Mid-training (§2.3) does three things. It progressively extends context from pre-training's 4K to 200K (in chunks: 1T tokens at 32K, 500B at 128K, 50B at 200K). It upsamples long documents and agentic trajectories at each later stage. And it dumps a 10-million issue-PR-pair software-engineering dataset — derived by relaxing the repo-filtering criteria from GLM-4.5 and strengthening per-issue quality filtering. The issue-PR portion alone is ∼160B unique tokens after filtering.

The specific trick I want to name: the repo-level concatenation. GLM-5 doesn't train SWE data as isolated diffs; it concatenates the commit diff, the GitHub issue text, the matching pull request, and the relevant source files into a single unified training sequence. This is how you get a base model that already understands "the repo context that contains this issue"; the follow-on RL stages then teach the model to navigate and edit that repo. Without this mid-training data, the RL stage would have to teach the model both repo-navigation and editing, and the cold-start gradient would be brutal.

Long-context data synthesis: the part that beats "lost in the middle"

Natural long documents — books, papers, long web articles — are abundant at 32K but thin past 128K. §2.3 names two recent synthesis recipes GLM-5 adapts: NextLong (random-doc packing with aggressive deduplication) and EntropyLong (predictive-uncertainty-guided packing). GLM-5's variant does similarity-interleaved packing: cluster short related documents by semantic similarity, then interleave them into one long sequence. The intuition is that the model has to hold competing semantic threads in memory to disambiguate which thread the next token belongs to — that's the hardest version of long-context attention, and dense synthetic packing of random unrelated docs doesn't force it.

At the 200K stage specifically, the team adds MRCR-like multi-round corefence data, generalizing OpenAI's MRCR paradigm: the model is shown many rounds of dialogue that each reference prior turns, and tested on whether it can recall the correct prior turn when asked. §2.3: “a subsequent 200K mid-training stage, building upon the initial 128K phase, further bolstered the model's performance even within the 128K context window.” That last clause is the interesting one — training at 200K improves 128K behavior, which argues the model at 128K was leaving capacity on the table that only 200K data unlocked. This is the opposite of the common “train at target length” intuition; for long-context, over-reaching during training beats matching the deployment window.

SFT: three thinking modes and an INT4-aware phase

SFT extends context to 202,752 tokens and introduces three thinking behaviors the paper takes the trouble to name individually:

  • Interleaved Thinking: the model emits a thinking block before every response and every tool call. The paper footnotes this as first introduced by Claude. I think the honest framing is: GLM-5 (and several 2025-2026 models) are adopting Anthropic's interleaved-extended-thinking pattern as the default, because it turns out to matter a lot for tool use. A tool call preceded by one sentence of reasoning about why that tool is almost always better than a tool call raw.
  • Preserved Thinking: in coding agent scenarios, the model retains all prior thinking blocks across turns rather than dropping them. The paper cites Claude Opus 4.5 as prior art. The pain point being solved: when the thinking is dropped at turn boundaries, the model re-derives reasoning it already had, wasting tokens and sometimes drifting. Preserved thinking cashes out as better consistency on long-horizon tasks — the same tasks where CC-Bench-V2's multi-step chained evaluation would otherwise punish the model.
  • Turn-level Thinking: per-turn control. Disable thinking on trivial “what time is it” style requests to save latency; enable it on complex ones. Implementation detail: the chat template exposes an enable_thinking flag that toggles a <think> prefix (chat_template.jinja on the pinned SHA).

Separately, the paper commits an INT4 QAT pass during SFT (§2.4.3). Every SFT example is trained under a quantization kernel that is bitwise-identical between training and inference. This is the sort of move that sounds small but compounds: it means the deployment-time INT4 quantization (for the MoE experts on Ascend, per §5) isn't a separate post-training step that might drift quality — it's the same numeric path the SFT itself saw. Released as GLM-5-FP8 separately from GLM-5 (BF16), and the HF model card has an FP8 sibling.

One more number from SFT worth calling out: coding-trajectory data is filtered such that erroneous segments within trajectories are retained but masked out in the loss function (§3.1). The design intent is teaching error-correction behavior without reinforcing incorrect actions. This is subtle enough to be easy to miss — you're not training the model to emit bad actions, but you are training it on sequences where a bad action appears in the context and the next action recovers. That shape is hard to synthesize and usually comes from real-world rollouts of a prior-generation agent.

reasoning rl // GRPO + IcePop, KL deleted

Post-training starts with Reasoning RL. The algorithm backbone is GRPO, augmented with IcePop — the 2025 technique for training-inference mismatch in MoE RL — but with one deliberate modification: the KL regularization term is removed. Let's walk the loss in full, paper §3.2 Equation 1:

$$ \mathcal{L}(\theta) = -\mathbb{E}_{x\sim\mathcal{D},\,\{y_i\}_{i=1}^G \sim \pi^{\text{infer}}_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(\cdot\mid x)} \left[ \frac{1}{G}\sum_{i=1}^{G}\frac{1}{|y_i|}\sum_{t=1}^{|y_i|} \operatorname{pop}(\rho_{i,t},\,1/\beta,\,\beta) \cdot \min\!\left( r_{i,t}\hat{A}_{i,t},\, \operatorname{clip}(r_{i,t},\,1-\epsilon_{\text{low}},\,1+\epsilon_{\text{high}})\hat{A}_{i,t} \right) \right] $$

Walk it symbol by symbol. $\pi^{\text{infer}}_{\theta_{\text{old}}}$ is the inference policy, the one that generated the trajectories; $\pi^{\text{train}}_{\theta_{\text{old}}}$ is the training policy that receives gradients. In a single-GPU world these would be identical, but in a scaled MoE with speculative decoding and kernel-level quantization differences between rollout runtime and training forward pass, they're not — and the gap is the first place agent RL goes off the rails. The ratio $\rho_{i,t} = \pi^{\text{train}}_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(y_{i,t}\mid\cdot) \,/\, \pi^{\text{infer}}_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(y_{i,t}\mid\cdot)$ measures that gap per token; $\operatorname{pop}(\rho, 1/\beta, \beta)$ is IcePop's “policy-ordering preservation” operator that clips this ratio to $[1/\beta, \beta]$ and passes its gradient — keeping training bounded when rollout and training don't agree.

$r_{i,t} = \pi_\theta(y_{i,t}\mid\cdot) \,/\, \pi^{\text{train}}_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(y_{i,t}\mid\cdot)$ is the usual PPO/GRPO importance ratio, clipped asymmetrically with $\epsilon_{\text{low}}$ and $\epsilon_{\text{high}}$ (paper doesn't report exact values; IcePop's defaults are typically asymmetric to penalize off-policy large positive updates more). $\hat{A}_{i,t}$ is the GRPO group advantage — the response-level reward minus the group mean, divided by group standard deviation, for the $G$ rollouts in the group.

The POP operator is the one piece most readers skim — and it's the piece doing real work. Picture what pop(rho, 1/beta, beta) looks like:

Notice the POP plot on the right is a plain hard clip — flat outside the band, identity inside. That's what "gradient-passing clip" means: inside [1/beta, beta] the operator is identity so the gradient of the outer PPO-style min-clip is what drives learning; outside the band the operator is constant so the entire token's gradient zeros out. It's the cleanest thing that works for training-inference drift: you don't try to correct the gap, you just refuse to backprop through it.

The pain point POP solves. In a scaled MoE with speculative decoding, FP8 compute, and rollout-side kernel fusions, the token distribution the inference-time sampler used is literally not the distribution the training-time forward pass would have produced — they use different kernels on the same weights. That's $\pi^{\text{infer}} \ne \pi^{\text{train}}$ even at fixed $\theta_{\text{old}}$. Without POP, the first few steps of RL are dominated by tokens whose $\rho$ is far from 1 simply because of numeric drift, and the gradient update direction is noise. With POP, those tokens pass zero gradient and the remaining in-band tokens carry the signal. Setting beta = infinity would mean "trust all tokens" (the naive mistake); setting beta = 1 would mean "trust no tokens" (no learning). The band is a tunable "how much numeric-drift tolerance do we want" knob.

paper vs IcePop: why drop the KL

Paper §3.2: "Compared to the original IcePop formulation, we remove the KL regularization term to accelerate RL improvement." The original IcePop notion page includes a KL penalty between the current policy and the reference policy to stabilize MoE RL. GLM-5 drops it. I read this as a bet that the POP clipping plus GRPO's group-relative advantage already provide sufficient stability — adding a KL term to reference would slow convergence, and since the pipeline already has cross-stage distillation as the anti-forgetting mechanism, a KL to reference is redundant. The trade-off is that a bad reward signal can pull the model further from reference before being caught. For verifiable reasoning tasks (math, code with unit tests), the reward signal is high-fidelity, so this trade-off pays. I'd expect the KL term to come back if they ever ran reward-model-based RL.

Reasoning data curation, the part people skip

One paragraph worth pausing on (§3.2). Reasoning RL data covers math, scientific reasoning, code, logical reasoning, and multidisciplinary STEM. The data sources include TACO (algorithmic code), NeMoTron-Math, AimO-2, Synthetic-2, and several GLM-internal sets. But the key move is difficulty-filtered data construction: the team retains only problems that are “challenging for the GLM-4.7 model.” The reason this framing matters is that the RL training data is tuned to the predecessor's difficulty frontier, which is the only well-defined frontier at training-time. Without this filter the gradient on easy problems would dominate and the model would waste RL budget re-learning what it already knew; because of it, the reward landscape is sharpest precisely where learning matters.

For code specifically, the reward is unit-test-grounded (not reward-model-graded), and any sample whose tests fail due to sandbox errors rather than model errors is dropped. This is small but load-bearing data hygiene — reward noise from environment flakiness is the other thing that kills agent RL in practice, and you have to strip it out at the sample level so that the group-relative advantage in GRPO is computed against a clean distribution.

Why delete the KL term and not just tune it down? Because the KL term in standard RL is there to prevent the policy from drifting so far from a reference that the reward signal goes non-stationary. In GLM-5's pipeline, the reference itself is about to change: Reasoning RL's checkpoint will be the Agentic RL stage's starting point, and then the Agentic RL checkpoint becomes General RL's starting point. A KL term to a fixed reference slows convergence without providing the stability it would provide in a single-stage run. The POP clipping on the training-inference ratio already bounds how far a single gradient step can move the policy, and the sequential pipeline handles stage-level stability via cross-stage distillation. So the KL term is redundant — deleted to avoid paying for a safeguard that two other mechanisms already provide.

agent rl & environments // the plumbing is the product

Reasoning RL is the clean case: one-shot problems with verifiable rewards. Agent RL is not. A long-horizon coding trajectory can run for tens of thousands of tokens across many tool calls; an 8-hour Claude-Code-harness rollout can emit thousands of function invocations. Three specific problems have to be solved to train on this kind of data, and §4.1 walks each.

Problem 1: staleness. Filtering trajectories that lag too far.

During an agent rollout of length $n$ tokens, if the policy parameters advance from $w_0$ to $w'$ before the rollout completes, the first tokens of the rollout were generated by $w_0$, the middle by something in between, and the tail by $w'$. Standard GRPO assumes all samples in a group come from the same policy; that's false for long rollouts. The fix: drop any sample where $w' - w_0 > \tau$, a predefined staleness threshold. Paper §4.1: “This removes trajectories that lag too far behind the current policy.” Note the grammar — this is sample-level filtering, not batch-level aggregation. A long rollout gets partially retained and partially dropped based on parameter-version drift.

Problem 2: environment collapse. Pad or drop groups.

When the reward source is a sandbox (Docker container, terminal env, Playwright browser), the sandbox can crash for reasons unrelated to the model. A Python import fails because pip had a transient error; a port bind races; a filesystem fills up. These failures look like low rewards to the naive algorithm, but they're not — they're noise. GLM-5's solution (§4.1): record the failure reason per sample, drop environment-collapse failures, and then handle the resulting incomplete group explicitly:

  • If the number of valid samples in a group is more than half of the group size, pad by repeating valid samples to recover the group.
  • If the number of valid samples is less than or equal to half, drop the entire group.

The half-threshold is the paper's default. It's a design choice: you want the advantage normalization (group mean / std) to be based on enough real samples that the variance estimate is reasonable. Below half, you're computing a group statistic on too few observations and the advantage signal becomes noisy; above half, repeating-pad is cheap and preserves the gradient direction.

Problem 3: KV-cache locality across rollout turns. DP-aware routing.

This is the infra piece that I suspect does more for throughput than any of the algorithmic tricks. In a multi-turn agent rollout, turns $2, 3, 4, \ldots$ all share the prefix from turn 1. Under naive Data Parallelism routing (round-robin), turn 2's prefill would land on a different DP rank than turn 1's and its KV cache would be cold. GLM-5 introduces a stateful routing layer that maps each rollout ID to a fixed DP rank via consistent hashing. All turns of a rollout land on the same rank; the KV cache stays local; prefill cost for turn $k$ is proportional to the incremental tokens, not the cumulative context. To prevent long-term rank imbalance, the hashing is combined with lightweight dynamic load rebalancing.

Paper §4.1: “As rollout length increases, prefill cost remains proportional to incremental tokens rather than total context length.” That sentence is the whole efficiency story at long-horizon scale. Without this, a 100-turn rollout would pay full-context prefill 100 times; with it, once. The cost saving scales linearly with turns, which means the longer your rollouts the more this trick pays.

Notice all four turns of rollout r_i land on rank 2 in the right panel — the KV cache accumulates on that rank, so turn k's prefill is bounded by the tokens added since turn k-1. In the naive panel every turn is cold: full-context prefill, every turn, on a different rank.

The environment fleet

The reward-generation surface is what separates a paper-RL run from an operational agent-RL run. GLM-5 builds four families of environments (§4.2):

SWE (10k+ envs) — RepoLaunch

Built from real Issue-PR pairs; the RepoLaunch framework auto-analyzes a repo's installation, generates test commands, and uses an LLM to write language-aware log-parsers that extract Fail-to-Pass and Pass-to-Pass tests. 9 languages: Python, Java, Go, C, C++, JavaScript, TypeScript, PHP, Ruby. The F2P/P2P split is what lets you score partial progress without gaming.

Terminal envs — Harbor

Two pipelines: seed-task synthesis with a construction-agent + refine-agent loop, and a Harbor-format web-corpus pipeline where a coding agent synthesizes a task grounded in a web page and is its own first-pass validator. Docker construction accuracy > 90%. This is how Terminal-Bench 2.0 scores get trained.

Search — Web Knowledge Graph

Starting from early-stage search agent trajectories, 2M+ URLs are parsed into a WKG; low-to-mid-frequency entities become seeds; multi-hop subgraphs get converted into multi-entity reasoning questions. Three-stage difficulty filtering removes tool-free-answerable and basic-search-answerable questions; a verification agent validates the remainder bidirectionally.

Slide generation — 3-level reward

HTML-based slides scored on three reward levels: static markup attributes (CSS rules), runtime-rendered DOM properties (bbox, layout), and visual perceptual features (whitespace patterns). Reward-hacking is caught by refining the renderer implementation to “eliminate exploitable loopholes” — a specific win over naive rule-based rewards.

Notice each panel has the same vertical anatomy — synth, task/sandbox, reward formula, pain-point, benchmark. The fleet is four reward sources that share one RL backbone (GRPO + IcePop); the hard work was getting the four rewards clean enough that the group-advantage normalization in GRPO doesn't drown in environment noise.

The BrowseComp inference-time trick (keep-recent-k + discard-all)

This one earns its own subsection because it's a number-moving inference-time idea, not a training idea, and it applies to any tool-using agent with a growing context. Paper §4.2.4 observed that “model accuracy degrades substantially under extremely long contexts (e.g., beyond 100k tokens).” Their fix is a two-layer context management strategy.

Notice the top row — the early turns' observations are dashed-red (replaced by a short placeholder), while every reasoning / action block is solid. That asymmetry is the whole design: the retrievable part gets folded, the non-retrievable part gets kept. Discard-all kicks in when even the folded history blows past T=32K.

  • Keep-recent-k: for a trajectory $(q, r_1, a_1, o_1, r_2, a_2, o_2, \ldots, r_n, a_n, o_n)$ — where $r_i$ is reasoning, $a_i$ is action, $o_i$ is observation — fold only the observations older than the most recent $k$ rounds: replace $o_i$ (for $i \leq n-k$) with the placeholder string “Tool result is omitted to save tokens.” Reasoning and actions from every past round are preserved. The paper sets $k=5$.
  • Hierarchical: combine keep-recent-k with DeepSeek-V3.2's Discard-all strategy. When total context exceeds $T=32K$ tokens, discard the entire tool-call history and restart with a fresh context, while continuing to apply keep-recent-k. Paper selects $T=32K$ via parameter search.

Any long-horizon search agent with a growing context will benefit from keep-recent-k-over-observations; the $k=5$ value is probably not universal but the pattern is. The specific reason it works is that observations are retrievable on demand by re-calling the tool, so dropping them from context is information-preserving at a communicative level — the model can always ask again — while reasoning and actions are not retrievable, so they must be kept. Fold the retrievable, keep the non-retrievable. One-line napkin.

on-policy cross-stage distillation // the anti-forgetting part I want to be loud about

The paper's §3.5 is short — and in the arXiv v2 HTML rendering I read, the section header gets lost in the markdown extraction — but the idea is the hinge of the whole post-training pipeline. Paper §1 summary phrases it: “we utilized On-Policy Cross-Stage Distillation throughout this process to prevent catastrophic forgetting, ensuring the model retains its sharp reasoning edge while becoming a robust generalist.” The references cited (MiniLLM, Thinking Machines Lab's on-policy distillation, MiMo-v2-flash, Qwen3) tell you the technique family.

Here's my read of the mechanism, constructed from the references + §3.5's intent even though the v2 HTML body of §3.5 didn't extract cleanly for me. You run Reasoning RL to convergence, getting a checkpoint $\pi_R$. You then start Agentic RL from $\pi_R$. Without anything else, Agentic RL's gradient will happily pull the weights away from reasoning-relevant directions because agent rewards are about tool calls and trajectory success, not math. Cross-stage distillation adds a distillation loss term where the teacher is $\pi_R$ (the previous stage's checkpoint) and the student is the current-stage policy, but the distillation is on-policy: the distillation targets are computed on the agent-RL trajectories themselves. So the student, mid-agent-training, sees: “on this exact agentic trajectory, here's what the reasoning-RL model would have said at each token; stay close to it while still optimizing your agent reward.”

This is the thing the Thinking Machines Lab blog post generalized: if you distill off-policy (on a fixed dataset of teacher outputs), the student learns to imitate the teacher's distribution and may never encounter the states its own policy visits. If you distill on-policy (the student generates rollouts, the teacher's per-token distribution is computed on those rollouts), the student is pulled toward the teacher exactly where the student actually goes. The analogous story for GLM-5 is stage-to-stage: the agent-RL student visits trajectory states that the reasoning-RL teacher never saw at training time, but the teacher still has opinions about what tokens to emit at those states, and those opinions are information worth preserving.

The whole sequence of three RL stages, then, looks like this: Reasoning RL produces $\pi_R$ from $\pi_{\text{SFT}}$. Agentic RL runs GRPO+IcePop on agent envs, starting from $\pi_R$, with a distillation term anchoring to $\pi_R$ on the agent trajectories — producing $\pi_A$. General RL runs human-preference optimization starting from $\pi_A$, again with cross-stage distillation anchoring to $\pi_A$ (and possibly $\pi_R$) on general-preference trajectories, producing the final $\pi_\text{GLM-5}$.

$$ \mathcal{L}_{\text{stage}_s}(\theta) \;=\; \underbrace{\mathcal{L}_{\text{RL}}(\theta; \mathcal{D}_s)}_{\text{GRPO+IcePop on stage-}s\text{ env}} \;+\; \lambda \;\cdot\; \underbrace{\mathbb{E}_{y \sim \pi_{\theta}(\cdot | x), x \in \mathcal{D}_s} \Big[ \mathrm{KL}\big( \pi_{\theta_{s-1}}(\cdot|x, y_{

That's the mechanism in one line. The left term is the usual RL objective on stage s's environment; the right term is a reverse-KL distillation to the previous-stage checkpoint, evaluated on the states the student policy actually visits during stage-s rollouts. Reverse KL — not forward — because the paper follows Thinking Machines Lab's on-policy distillation, where reverse KL's mode-seeking behavior is a feature: the student is pulled toward the teacher's high-probability actions on the student's own states, not averaged across the teacher's support.

Notice the green dashed arrow — the reverse-KL gradient flows back to the student only. The teacher is frozen; the student's rollouts visit the states; the teacher's opinions land exactly where the student is, not where a fixed dataset happens to sample. That's the whole mechanism; everything else is bookkeeping.

the napkin version

Sequential RL is how you avoid reward-function overloading — you can't write one reward that covers math, tool calls, and human preference at the same time without the reward becoming a mushy weighted sum that doesn't optimize anything crisply. But the cost of sequential RL is catastrophic forgetting: stage 2's gradient updates don't know or care about stage 1's benefit. On-policy cross-stage distillation is the fix: at each stage, distill on the trajectories the current stage actually visits, toward the prior-stage policy, so the prior's knowledge gets re-encoded into the current-stage weights rather than overwritten. I think this is a more general recipe than its appearance in GLM-5 suggests, and I'd bet we see it show up in Kimi K3 and Qwen4-family post-training recipes.

2. chat_template.jinja — three thinking modes as jinja control flow

The paper's §3.1 names three thinking behaviors — Interleaved, Preserved, and Turn-level — as SFT innovations. The shipped chat_template.jinja implements them as three jinja branches wrapped around the system prompt and message loop. Here's the load-bearing fragment, paraphrased and elided: 

# chat_template.jinja @ SHA 4e6698b — thinking-mode gating

{%- set enable_thinking = enable_thinking | default(true) %}
{%- set preserve_thinking = preserve_thinking | default(true) %}

{# ... system prompt rendering ... #}

{%- for message in messages %}
  {%- if message.role == "user" %}
    <|user|>{{ message.content }}
  {%- elif message.role == "assistant" %}
    <|assistant|>
    {%- if message.thinking and preserve_thinking %}
      # Preserved Thinking: keep prior thinking blocks across turns
      <think>{{ message.thinking }}</think>
    {%- endif %}
    {{ message.content }}
    {%- if message.tool_calls %}
      # Interleaved Thinking: tool calls follow a thinking block
      {%- for tc in message.tool_calls %}
        <|tool_call|>{{ tc.function.name }}{{{ tc.function.arguments }}}
      {%- endfor %}
    {%- endif %}
  {%- endif %}
{%- endfor %}

# Turn-level Thinking: inference-time toggle
{%- if add_generation_prompt %}
  <|assistant|>
  {%- if enable_thinking %}<think>{%- endif %}
{%- endif %}

Three observations. First, Turn-level Thinking is the enable_thinking flag — when true, the generation prompt is opened with a <think> so the model starts in the thinking state; when false, the generation prompt goes straight to the main output. That's why latency-sensitive calls can skip reasoning: no prefix, no encouragement. Second, Preserved Thinking is the preserve_thinking flag — when true, past-turn assistant thinking blocks are re-rendered in context so the model doesn't re-derive its own priors. Third, Interleaved Thinking is structural: every tool call in an assistant message is preceded by the assistant's <think> block, so the model always emits a reason for why this tool. The pain point all three are solving is the same one: agent performance degrades when reasoning is invisible or fragmented.

5. DSA kernel in modeling_glm_moe_dsa.py — the inference-time Python

The modeling file isn't on the official Hub (the zai-org/GLM-5 GitHub repo is deployment docs, not modeling code), but a shared modeling_glm_moe_dsa.py ships with the weights for trust_remote_code=True loading. The DSA kernel, re-expressed with shape comments against the config above: 

# Per-layer DSA attention, paraphrased from the shipped modeling code.
# Shapes use the config fields: B=batch, L_q=query length (usually 1 at decode),
# L_ctx=context length (up to 202,752), H=64, D=256, h=32, d=128, r_kv=512.

class DSAAttention(nn.Module):
    def forward(self, hidden, layer_kv_cache):
        # --- [1] MLA projections (unchanged from a dense MLA layer) ---
        q = self.W_UQ(self.W_DQ(hidden)).view(B, L_q, H, D)   # H=64, D=256
        c_kv = self.W_DKV(hidden)                             # [B, L_q, r_kv=512]

        # Append new c_kv to the layer cache; cache lives as the LATENT only.
        layer_kv_cache = torch.cat([layer_kv_cache, c_kv], dim=1)  # [B, L_ctx, 512]

        # --- [2] Lightning indexer on the latent cache ---
        # Query-side: project hidden -> 32 heads of 128 dims.
        idx_q = self.W_iQ(hidden).view(B, L_q, 32, 128)
        # Key-side: project the LATENT (not the full KV) -> same shape.
        idx_k = self.W_iK(layer_kv_cache).view(B, L_ctx, 32, 128)
        idx_q, idx_k = apply_rope(idx_q, idx_k)               # interleave on the last 64 dims

        # Per-head dot product, ReLU, sum across heads.
        scores = torch.einsum("blhd,bmhd->blhm", idx_q, idx_k)   # [B, L_q, 32, L_ctx]
        scores = F.relu(scores).sum(dim=2)                       # [B, L_q, L_ctx]

        # --- [3] Top-K: pick 2048 positions per query ---
        topk = scores.topk(k=2048, dim=-1).indices               # [B, L_q, 2048]

        # --- [4] Sparse MLA attention on those 2048 positions ---
        # Gather K, V only where the indexer said to look. K,V up-projections are
        # standard MLA: W_UK takes r_kv=512 to H*192 nope + a separate 64-dim K_rope
        # from hidden (not from the latent); W_UV takes r_kv=512 to H*256.
        return sparse_mla_attention(q, layer_kv_cache, topk)

The thing to notice: the indexer reads layer_kv_cache, which is the 512-dim latent — never the full-dim K. That's what makes the 1,000-step warmup work: the module being trained is the projection from r_kv=512 into 32 heads of 128, which is 2.1M params per layer (plus the query-side projection from 6,144 into 32x128). Small module, strong gradient signal, 1k steps suffice. If the indexer had to read the full key (which would be H*256 = 16,384 dims), it would be much bigger and the warmup wouldn't converge nearly as fast. The architectural decision to keep the cache as the latent isn't just about memory — it's what makes the indexer tractable.

infrastructure // the engineering under the model

Four infra choices in the paper move numbers. I won't cover all seven NPU platforms — that's ecosystem work, not insight. What I'll dig into are the four that have leverage beyond "GLM-5 runs on our chip": async RL with rollout/train decoupling, INT4 QAT bitwise-identical training/serving, W4A8 on Ascend with MLAPO fusion, and the Lightning Indexer kernel. Each one exists because a specific cost would otherwise dominate.

Async RL: decoupling rollouts from training updates

Classical on-policy RL has a synchronization point: the rollout workers generate a batch, the training workers consume it, the policy updates, repeat. At long-horizon agent-RL scale, that synchronization is the bottleneck — a single slow rollout (the one stuck in a 10-minute Docker build) stalls the training step for everyone. GLM-5 decouples them: rollout workers run continuously against the currently serving inference policy $\pi^{\text{infer}}_{\theta_{\text{old}}}$, training workers pull any completed rollouts and update the training policy $\pi_\theta$ in parallel. The gap between these two policies is exactly what IcePop's POP operator is designed to tolerate — see the RL section for the ratio-clip picture.

The infrastructure is built on top of THUDM/slime, the async RL framework introduced in GLM-4.5. New in GLM-5 is the DP-aware routing layer (see agent RL section) that pins every request from a given rollout to the same DP rank — the KV cache stays warm, prefill scales with incremental tokens, not cumulative context.

INT4 QAT: train and serve share one numeric path

Notice the right panel's middle box — "SFT bwd (STE)". That's the thing that makes INT4 QAT work: backward uses straight-through estimation to pass gradients through the quantize-dequantize, while forward uses the actual INT4 kernel. Train and serve share numerics; the optimizer alone holds BF16 precision.

Mixed-precision on Ascend: W8A8 for attention, W4A8 for MoE experts

A point worth naming precisely, because most readers (and I admit, my first draft) collapse it to "W4A8 everywhere". Paper §5 is explicit: standard Attention and MLP blocks use W8A8 (INT8 weights, INT8 activations); MoE experts use W4A8 (INT4 weights, INT8 activations). The rationale is memory-asymmetric — the 256 routed experts carry the bulk of the 744B parameter budget, so 4-bit quantization there dominates the memory savings; attention and the shared expert stay at 8-bit because they're accuracy-sensitive and their share of total params is small.

Outlier suppression uses QuaRot rotations; scaling calibration uses Flex_AWQ_SSZ. The combination gets the 754B BF16 model onto a single Atlas 800T A3 machine, which is the deployment-density target the paper is optimizing for.

MLAPO: fusing 13 pre-processing ops into one super operator

The MLA prefill path on Ascend needs 13 small pre-processing ops — RMSNorm, Q/K/V LoRA down-projections, RoPE setup on the rope-carrying heads, the latent-cache write, the nope/rope split, and several reshape/transpose steps. Before MLAPO these ran as separate kernel launches; each launch pays a device-side scheduling cost that's disproportionate for tiny tensors. MLAPO fuses them into a single kernel that uses Ascend's Vector unit for the normalization/reshape work and the Cube unit for the matmuls in parallel — the two unit types are physically distinct on Ascend silicon, so "fuse" here means "schedule them concurrently inside one kernel", not just "inline into a loop".

Lightning Indexer kernel

The DSA indexer's math is simple — einsum, ReLU, sum, top-K — but at 200K context length, naive PyTorch eats latency on the einsum and the topk. The Lightning Indexer kernel fuses the three into one pass: project queries and keys, score with ReLU-gated dot product, select top-2048, all without materializing the full [L_q, L_ctx] score tensor in HBM. At decode (L_q=1), the kernel is dominated by the L_ctx-wide memory read; at prefill, it parallelizes across query positions. This is the specific kernel the paper §5.2 cites as necessary to make DSA's theoretical FLOP savings turn into wall-clock savings — without it, the indexer's memory bandwidth would eat the compute savings from sparse attention.

Why all four choices cluster. They're not independent wins; they're four sides of the same cost-avoidance story. Async RL avoids the cost of sync-waiting on the slowest rollout; DP-aware routing avoids cold KV caches; INT4 QAT avoids post-hoc quantization drift; MLAPO avoids launch overhead on the hot attention path. None of them are new ideas on their own — async RL is in OpenRLHF, DP-aware routing is in vLLM's PagedAttention, INT4 QAT is LLM.int8()-era work, and kernel fusion is CUDA 101. What's new is that the recipe assembles all four for MLA + DSA + MoE + multi-turn agent RL, and the pieces compose rather than fight each other. If any one of them were missing, the other three would degrade.

takeaways // signed off

[01] Muon Split ends the MuonClip era

Kimi K2's MuonClip clipped per-head max logits at training time to tame Muon-induced blow-up on a GQA MoE. GLM-5's Muon Split does it structurally: orthogonalize per head rather than per projection matrix, and the blow-up never happens. I think Muon Split will become the default Muon recipe for MLA models published in the second half of 2026 — it's cheaper, it's a one-line code change, and it removes a whole category of training-stability fire. The MuonClip story was a fix for a symptom; Muon Split is a fix for the cause.

[02] DSA-via-CPT is the scale-efficient long-context path

The 20B-token DSA adaptation result is the most consequential thing in this paper that isn't about post-training. If you're building an MoE and want long context without paying the DeepSeek-V3.2 adaptation tax, the recipe is: train MLA through mid-training, warm up a small indexer on frozen weights, then jointly train backbone+indexer for a small fraction of your pre-training budget. 1/47 the tokens, close-to-zero quality loss. I'd expect this to be the standard pattern for 2026's open-weight frontier models.

[03] The three-stage RL pipeline is the interesting object

Everyone can copy GRPO+IcePop. Everyone can write a 10k-environment SWE fleet — with enough RepoLaunch engineering. The thing that's actually hard to copy is the sequence: Reasoning RL → Agentic RL → General RL with on-policy cross-stage distillation holding the knowledge chain together. The paper is admirably structural about this: “On-policy cross-stage distillation throughout… to prevent catastrophic forgetting.” I think this is the insight that distinguishes competent RL post-training from the accidental “we forgot the model could do math after we RL'd it on agents” story.

[04] Inference-time wins are underweighted in the discourse

Keep-recent-k + discard-all adds 20+ points on BrowseComp for free. Not a parameter change, not a training-time change, not a data change. This is the pattern: as context-length-sensitive evaluations proliferate, inference-time context management is going to matter as much as architecture. The signal to watch in 2026 benchmarks is the spread between vanilla-inference numbers and context-managed numbers; the models that don't publish both are hiding a load-bearing part of their story.

[05] The open-weights story is real and worth defending

Placing this piece next to Qwen3.5-Omni on the index: Qwen went closed, GLM-5 went open under MIT. Two roughly-contemporary Chinese labs made opposite release decisions. GLM-5's decision means people can read the config.json, poke at the chat template, reproduce the MTP behavior, train LoRAs on the base. The ecosystem visible around this repo — 10+ adapter finetunes, 38 downstream finetunes, 26 quantized variants within two months — is the tangible value of open weights. I'm going to name this out loud: the open-weights commitment is what makes this model interesting beyond its benchmark numbers.

[06] What I wanted from the paper but didn't get

Three ablations I wish were public: (a) the full 6-length-bucket RULER table for 744B GLM-5 with/without DSA — Paper Table 3 shows 128K numbers on four retrieval benchmarks (see §dsa), but the length-sweep for the full 744B isn't there; the Flash-size surrogate is all we get at lengths < 128K. (c) An explanation for the GSM8K/MATH base-model regression from 4.5→5. All three matter for reproducibility; none of them are load-bearing for the paper's main argument, but they'd elevate it from "we did it" to "here's why it works."
