Kimi K2 · Why 85 on Creative Writing Isn’t Luck

MuonClip stabilizes the 1T MoE base, but the real story is in post-training: a 23000-tool agentic data factory, a closed-loop self-critique rubric reward. This article follows that single thread.

1T / 32B · MoE MuonClip τ=100 Self-Critique Rubric 23k Tools
overview architecture muonclip data factory rl loop evaluation code takeaways
TL;DR

Kimi K2 is a 1.04T-total / 32B-active MoE — essentially "DeepSeek-V3 with half the heads". The pre-training headliner is MuonClip, using qk-clip to tame Muon's exploding attention logits: 15.5T tokens without a single loss spike. But what actually opens the gap is the post-training pipeline: 3000+ real MCP tools plus 20000+ synthetic ones, funnelled through an LLM judge to reject-sample agentic trajectories; a closed loop between verifiable reward and self-critique rubric reward — the critic is continuously recalibrated by on-policy rollouts on verifiable tasks, then used to score subjective ones. The eye-popping 85.0 on Creative Writing isn't an accident; it's what falls out of that loop.

overview // why this paper matters

My first reaction to Kimi K2 was "another 1T MoE". By the time I closed the tech report, I felt differently: this is less a paper and more Moonshot open-sourcing their entire post-training playbook. The architecture — MLA attention, 384 experts choose 8, 61 layers — is basically a DeepSeek-V3 re-parameterization. The actual product is §3.

So this article's structure deliberately doesn't mirror the paper's. I'll cover the base model quickly, spend real ink on MuonClip (the one genuinely new optimizer-level contribution), and reserve the longest stretch for agentic data synthesis and closed-loop RL. That's the real heart of K2.

[base] 1T / 32B MoE

61 layers (1 dense + 60 MoE), hidden 7168, MLA attention, 384 experts with top-8 routing (sparsity 48), fp8 E4M3 storage. Versus DeepSeek-V3: half the heads, 50% more experts, 13% fewer activated params.

[pretrain] MuonClip + 15.5T

Muon hits a wall of exploding attention logits at scale — Moonshot's answer is qk-clip: derive a per-head scaling factor from the max logit and rewrite W_q/W_k after every step. The entire 15.5T-token run has zero loss spikes.

[post] agentic factory + closed RL

23k+ tools × thousands of agents × multi-turn trajectories × LLM-judge filtering feed SFT; then RL uses verifiable rewards to recalibrate a critic that grades the subjective tasks. That's why K2 jumps on creative writing and multi-turn tool use simultaneously.

Notice the three lanes are not equal in novelty: [A] architecture is a re-parameterization of DeepSeek-V3 (same class), [B] MuonClip is the one-post-update-loop fix Moonshot actually invented, and [C] post-training is where Moonshot's moat lives. The cover shape of the article follows that asymmetry — short on [A], medium on [B], longest on [C].

One line: architecture is inherited, the base is stabilized by MuonClip, the novel work lives in §3. The rest of this article follows that order.

architecture // the base, briefly

I'll only answer three questions about the base: 1. what did they change vs DeepSeek-V3; 2. why; 3. is it verifiable from config.json.

The reason the base deserves so little space is that nothing in the architecture is new. K2 inherits MLA, noaux_tc sigmoid routing, and even the DeepseekV3ForCausalLM modeling class verbatim. What K2 picked is a specific set of five knob positions to differ on — halved heads, bumped experts, shorter dense prefix, higher rope_theta, Muon-over-AdamW. Each of these is a one-line config edit; the contribution is the combination, not the pieces. Without this framing the rest of the section reads as trivia; with it, every number is a comparative move.

1. MLA + 384 experts + halved heads

K2's architecture class is literally DeepseekV3ForCausalLM — the HF config.json copies it verbatim and mainly changes three numbers: experts 256 → 384 (sparsity 8 → 48), attention heads 128 → 64, dense prefix 3 layers → 1 layer. MLA knobs (q_lora_rank=1536, kv_lora_rank=512, v_head_dim=128) are kept; rope θ is bumped from DeepSeek-V3's 10000 to 50000 (one-line change worth flagging).

2. Why cut heads, add experts

Paper §2.3 is refreshingly blunt: at 128k context (table stakes for agentic use cases), doubling heads from 64 to 128 only buys 0.5%–1.2% validation-loss improvement while costing 83% more FLOPs. Going the other direction on experts, sparsity 8 → 48 saves 1.69×–1.15× FLOPs at iso-loss. So Moonshot makes a very utilitarian trade: move compute from attention to MoE. This, I think, is the base's nicest design call.

Notice the five DELTAS down the center — attention halved, experts +50%, dense prefix from 3 to 1, rope_theta up 5x, optimizer swapped to Muon. Every one of those changes is a one-line config edit on top of DeepSeek-V3's skeleton; K2's contribution is not the skeleton, it's picking which knobs to move.

paper vs HF drift

Paper §2.3 lists "Activated Parameters = 32.6B"; the abstract and HF card both round to "32B"; paper §4.2 Table 4 totals 1043B. Counting HF safetensors yields 1026470731056 ≈ 1026.5B total — about 1.6% shy of the paper number (a 16.5B-parameter gap). My guess: the paper counts theoretical non-quantized parameters, HF counts what actually fits on disk after fp8 packing. For this article I'll use the paper's "1.04T / 32B active"; don't be surprised if the card says 32B or community repos count 1026.5B.

3. The pre-training numbers worth remembering

Paper §2.5 is specific about the schedule — WSD (Warmup–Stable–Decay): 500-step warmup, then 10T tokens at a flat 2e-4, then 5.5T with cosine decay to 2e-5; batch size held at 67M tokens throughout, weight decay 0.1. An annealing phase continues, lr 2e-5 → 7e-6, over 400B tokens @ 4k followed by 60B tokens @ 32k; YaRN then extends context to 128k.

The one hardware number worth keeping: H800 clusters, 16-way PP + 16-way EP + ZeRO-1 DP, ~30 GB per GPU for params/grads/optimizer and the rest for activations; MoE up-proj and SwiGLU inputs are stored in FP8 but never computed in FP8. The "FP8 storage, not FP8 compute" split strikes me as a pragmatic call — it saves memory without trading off model quality.

muonclip // keeping Muon stable at 1T

Moonshot's 2024 Moonlight work already showed Muon outperforms AdamW on token efficiency. But pushing Muon to 1T scale hit a wall: attention logits blow up past 1000. The paper's phrasing is unusually honest: "an issue that occurs more frequently with Muon but less with AdamW". That sentence landed hard, because it means anyone who wants to use Muon at scale has to solve this first.

Existing fixes are inadequate: logit soft-capping (the Gemma approach) only clips pre-softmax — the q·k product itself can overflow before capping; QK-Norm is the natural idea, but MLA never materializes a full K matrix — the KV is compressed at inference, so there's nowhere to plug in a per-key norm. You have to attack the weights themselves.

qk-clip, in one line

The idea fits in one line: monitor the max logit per batch; if it exceeds threshold τ, rescale W_q and W_k proportionally. Crucially, this is post-hoc weight rescaling — it does not alter the current step's forward/backward, it's purely a cleanup action.

The per-head version (what Algorithm 1 actually runs):

$$ S_{\max}^{h} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{d}} \max_{\mathbf{X}\in B}\ \max_{i,j}\ \mathbf{Q}_i^h {\mathbf{K}_j^h}^\top,\qquad \gamma_h = \min\!\left(1,\ \frac{\tau}{S_{\max}^{h}}\right) $$

Then for MLA specifically, only the head-specific parts get rescaled; the shared rotary key is left alone:

$$ \mathbf{W}_{qc}^{h} \leftarrow \sqrt{\gamma_h}\,\mathbf{W}_{qc}^{h},\quad \mathbf{W}_{kc}^{h} \leftarrow \sqrt{\gamma_h}\,\mathbf{W}_{kc}^{h},\quad \mathbf{W}_{qr}^{h} \leftarrow \gamma_h\,\mathbf{W}_{qr}^{h},\quad \mathbf{W}_{kr}\ \text{unchanged} $$

Why γ_h on q_r, √γ_h split between q_c and k_c? Because k_r is shared across heads — touching it would cause cross-head interference. Since the key-side rotary is off-limits, the query side has to absorb the full γ on its own; you can no longer split equally with the key. This is an MLA-specific subtlety; missing it would make reproducing qk-clip very confusing.

a paper-vs-blog discrepancy

Both the blog and paper lead with an α-version: $q_i = \eta^\alpha W_q x_i,\ k_i = \eta^{1-\alpha} W_k x_i$, noting α is "typically set to 0.5". But Algorithm 1 directly uses per-head γ_h and √γ — q_c / k_c get √γ (equivalent to α = 0.5), while q_r gets the full γ (equivalent to α = 1). In other words, the "balancing α" is not a single global hyperparameter in the actual implementation; it's MLA's sub-components being treated differently. I spent a good twenty minutes reconciling the formulas with the pseudocode — flagging.

The final MuonClip step just adds a post-update loop on top of Muon:

# Algorithm 1, paper-level pseudocode. Not from a released repo.
for step in range(num_steps):
    # -- 1. Muon update (unchanged from Moonlight) --
    for W in trainable_weights:  # W in R^{n x m}
        M = mu * M + grad(W)                                  # momentum
        O = newton_schulz(M) * sqrt(max(n, m)) * 0.2          # match Adam RMS
        W -= lr * (O + wd * W)                                # decoupled wd

    # -- 2. QK-Clip (new) --
    for layer in model.layers:
        for h in layer.attn.heads:
            S_max_h = layer.attn.max_logit[h]                 # captured during fwd
            if S_max_h > tau:                                  # tau = 100 for K2
                gamma = tau / S_max_h
                W_qc[h] *= sqrt(gamma)   # head-specific query compressed
                W_kc[h] *= sqrt(gamma)   # head-specific key  compressed
                W_qr[h] *= gamma         # head-specific query rotary (full γ)
                # W_kr is SHARED across heads — do NOT touch

MuonClip as a post-update scaffold

Notice stage 2 is a forward-pass observation, not a reforward — the max logit is captured during the same forward that computed the gradient. Stage 4 rescales weights in-place after Muon has already stepped them; it doesn't undo the step, only the pathological growth direction. That's why MuonClip is a scaffold (stage 4 becomes a no-op once the model is stable) rather than a permanent regularizer.

What it looks like in practice

Paper Figure 2 (right) is worth looking up — at the start, the max logit pins to τ = 100 and tracks the ceiling exactly, then after ~30% of training steps it voluntarily drops to a lower stable range, as if the model itself learns not to explode. This means qk-clip is a tight constraint early on and effectively dormant later — it's scaffolding, not a permanent regularizer. The paper is very understated about this, but it's the most interesting sentence in §2.1 to me.

Notice where the green curve detaches from the tau=100 line — roughly 30% into the run, the model has learned a weight configuration where the rescale is no longer needed and the per-head gamma drops below 1 spontaneously. The scaffold goes dormant; the training continues on Muon proper. Contrast the dashed red, where the unclipped logit marches past 1000 and training NaNs shortly after.

Together with this, K2's loss curve (paper Figure 3) has zero spikes across the entire 15.5T-token run — the paper explicitly labels it "per-step, without smoothing or sub-sampling". Not strictly monotonic (normal stochastic noise is still there), but the kind of unpredictable divergence that haunts industrial-scale training has been suppressed by a single mechanism. This is what stuck with me after §2.1: a paper-worthy contribution can be as small as "one post-update loop".

the napkin takeaway

MuonClip is the moonshot.ai-era fix for a specific pain point: Muon's joint-spectrum orthogonalization pushes individual heads to grow weights when they want sharper attention, and at 1T scale this overshoots. The follow-up fix in GLM-5 — Muon Split — attacks the same root cause structurally by orthogonalizing each per-head sub-block independently, so the blow-up never happens in the first place. Read the two together: K2 treated the symptom, GLM-5 treated the cause. That's how this kind of research moves.

agentic data factory // where post-training starts

Ordinary instruction data is ordinary — K2 uses K1.5 plus in-house expert models plus LLM judging, same as anyone else. What's actually worth dissecting is §3.1.1 agentic data synthesis. That's the section where Moonshot spends real budget on the phrase "agentic intelligence".

One pipeline, three stages

flowchart LR A[Tool Spec Repo
3k real MCP + 20k synth] --> B[Agent + Task Gen
thousands of agents · rubric tasks] B --> C[Trajectory Gen
User sim + Tool sim + Agent] C --> D[LLM Judge
filter against rubric] D --> E[SFT / Rejection Sample] F[Real Sandbox
k8s · 10k concurrent] --> E

Notice the Real Sandbox arrow lands at SFT, not at the Judge — for code / SWE tasks the k8s unit-test execution is a second, harder filter after LLM-judge rubric passes. The simulator produces look-plausible trajectories; the sandbox catches look-plausible-but-wrong code. Two filters in series, different axes.

01
Tool Spec Generation

Crawl 3000+ real MCP tools from GitHub (specs included), then evolve 20000+ synthetic ones via hierarchical domain generation — pick a category (financial trading, software apps, robot control…), evolve sub-domains, synthesize tool specs per sub-domain. The t-SNE viz shows the two tool populations covering complementary regions with almost no overlap.

02
Agent + Rubric Task

Sample toolsets ("you are an agent with X, Y, Z"), synthesize thousands of agents with different system prompts. Each agent gets multiple tasks, each task paired with a rubric — success criteria, expected tool-use pattern, evaluation checkpoints. Everything flows through rubrics; no free-text judging.

03
Trajectory Sim

LLMs play personas (distinct communication styles and preferences), LLMs play the tool environment ("functionally equivalent to a world model" — stateful, with controlled stochasticity for partial failures and edge cases), and the agent runs multi-turn dialogue. Massive trajectory generation.

04
LLM-Judge Filter

A separate LLM judge scores every trajectory against the rubric, retaining only successes. This is large-scale rejection sampling in a fancy coat. For coding / SWE tasks, paper §3.1.1 adds real execution sandboxes on top (the scale number lives in §3.2.1: 10000+ concurrent Kubernetes instances, the same infra that also serves RL unit tests) — the simulator's fidelity isn't enough for code.

Why this pipeline scales

Because every stage is driven by an LLM — tool specs written by LLMs, agent prompts sampled by LLMs, users simulated by LLMs, tool environments simulated by LLMs, judging done by LLMs. With compute, throughput is bounded only by inference speed — unlike early RLHF, which bottlenecked on human annotators.

Cost: simulation fidelity

The paper admits this: "inherent limitation of simulation fidelity". Pure simulation teaches "looks like tool-use" patterns rather than actual problem-solving. That's why they absolutely need the k8s sandbox for real code — coding is the agentic task that exposes fake reasoning most quickly; real-execution feedback is irreplaceable.

One observation I can't shake

Rubrics thread through all of K2 post-training — agentic data is rubric-scored, RL self-critique is rubric-scored. My read: the rubric is Moonshot's answer to "scalable preference labels". RLHF ran out of human labels; Constitutional AI replaced them with principles; K2 replaces them with rubrics. The common move is reducing "what's a good answer" to an enumerable, checkable list. The difference: rubrics are more task-specific than principles — one rubric per task is easier to maintain than one big constitution. I think this pattern generalizes.

closed-loop rl // verifiable reward ↔ self-critique rubric

This is the section I marked up most. K2's RL system unifies verifiable and subjective tasks in a single gym, held together by a closed-loop critic: verifiable rewards continuously recalibrate a critic model, which in turn judges creative writing, open-ended QA, and everything else that resists rule-verification.

1. Verifiable Rewards Gym

§3.2.1 lists a wide verifiable-task inventory — math / STEM / logic (structured-data reasoning, 24-game, Sudoku, Morse decoding and friends); complex instruction following (code-interpreter checks + LLM-as-judge + an anti-hack layer); faithfulness (a sentence-level judge model); competitive coding + software engineering (k8s running unit tests); safety (attack model vs. target vs. judge, binary pass/fail).

a small design note Math / STEM prompts are filtered to moderate difficulty, using the SFT model's pass@k to estimate. Too easy → no signal; too hard → never positive reward. Every frontier lab is doing this quietly; few papers state it so plainly.

2. Self-Critique Rubric Reward

The subjective-task path (§3.2.2) goes like this: the same K2 — not a separate critic model, but a judge capability bootstrapped during SFT by mixing in preference datasets — switches into judge mode to score actor outputs pairwise. The rubric stack has three layers —

[F.1] Core Rubrics

Three "Kimi values": Clarity & Relevance (concise, on-topic), Conversational Fluency (natural turn-taking), Objective & Grounded (no meta-commentary, no flattery). These are negotiable against the model's own priors.

[F.2] Prescriptive (anti-hack)

Hard bans on common reward-hacking shortcuts: no opening flattery ("Good question!"), no explicit self-justification about why the answer is good. Looks minor, but these are the exact shortcuts RL would otherwise exploit.

[custom] Task rubrics

Hand-crafted task-specific rubrics from the data team. For creative writing, a rubric might forbid cliché openings; for a tool-calling task, it might require argument names to match a schema exactly.

3. Closed loop — the critic learns alongside the policy

This is K2's genuine contribution. A pure rubric-only RL run faces a drift problem — after a few policy updates, the policy's output distribution has shifted but the critic is still grading against the old style. Moonshot's fix is to use on-policy rollouts from verifiable tasks to update the critic as well: the critic must stay accurate on tasks that have ground truth, which amounts to giving it a "grounding anchor".

Notice the two flows into the critic — the blue arrow from [A] refines the critic on verifiable-task rollouts, the pink arrow from [B] queries it on subjective-task rollouts. That asymmetric traffic is the loop: the critic spends its gradients on ground-truth-grounded updates, then spends its forward passes on ground-truth-ungrounded judgments. The policy update at the bottom consumes both kinds of reward through the same K1.5 squared-loss objective.

Put plainly: [A] keeps the critic honest; [B] feeds critic scores into actor updates. One actor, one critic, one gym. The paper's phrasing — "a way of using verifiable rewards to improve the estimation of non-verifiable rewards" — took me three readings to appreciate. That one line is the center of §3.2.

4. Objective: the K1.5 squared loss

K2's policy optimization inherits K1.5's objective verbatim: sample K responses per prompt, center-subtract the reward, subtract a KL-like log-ratio regularizer, square. Not GRPO, not PPO — K1.5's own flavor.

$$ \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{RL}}(\theta)=\mathbb{E}_{x\sim\mathcal{D}}\!\left[\frac{1}{K}\sum_{i=1}^{K}\!\Big(r(x,y_i)-\bar{r}(x)-\tau\log\frac{\pi_\theta(y_i|x)}{\pi_{\mathrm{old}}(y_i|x)}\Big)^{\!2}\right] $$

Here $\bar{r}(x) = \frac{1}{K}\sum_i r(x,y_i)$ is the K-sample mean (baseline), $\tau > 0$ controls the KL regularization (note: this $\tau$ is a different quantity from the qk-clip threshold $\tau=100$ in §pretrain — the paper reuses the symbol). The loss absorbs both verifiable r and critic-scored r̂ seamlessly — from the actor's perspective, where the reward comes from doesn't matter. That's why joint RL barely adds complexity to the framework.

5. Three stability tricks

Budget Control

Per-task, per-sample token budget; overshoot → truncate + negative reward. Directly targets "RL makes outputs balloon" — longer outputs might pay off for reasoning, but elsewhere they're pure cost. The most pragmatic move in §3.2.3.

PTX Loss

The InstructGPT-style PTX loss returns in K2 — mix a hand-picked high-quality dataset into the RL objective to prevent RL from forgetting core knowledge. Old-school move, still universally used, because it still works.

Temperature Decay

High temperature early (for exploration), low temperature late (for stability). One schedule through the whole RL phase — essentially the compromise of handling creative and stability-sensitive tasks with one model.

6. Infra: broadcasting 1T weights in 30 seconds

The main difference between RL and SFT is that inference engines must absorb policy updates frequently. For a 1T model, moving new weights from trainer to inferencer is itself a systems problem. Per §3.3.2, they built a distributed checkpoint engine (MoonshotAI/checkpoint-engine) that streams parameter updates pipeline-style, bypassing any network filesystem — a full parameter refresh lands in under 30 seconds. An RL iteration is typically much longer than that, so switching overhead disappears.

Agentic rollout gets its own handling — because some rollouts are very long (multi-turn tool calls), partial rollout pauses long-tail trajectories for the next RL iteration. Heavy environments (VMs, code interpreters) are deployed as standalone services for easy scaling. None of this is paper-worthy on its own, but it's what lets agentic RL actually run on a 1T model.

    "163588": {"content": "<|im_system|>",     "special": true},
    "163601": {"content": "<|tool_calls_section_begin|>", "special": true},
    "163602": {"content": "<|tool_calls_section_end|>",   "special": true},
    "163603": {"content": "<|tool_call_begin|>",          "special": true},
    "163604": {"content": "<|tool_call_argument_begin|>", "special": true},
    "163605": {"content": "<|tool_call_end|>",            "special": true}
  }
}

The K2 tokenizer reserves five contiguous slots (163601–163605) for a structured tool-call schema: a section-level open/close pair plus per-call begin / argument-begin / end. The model emits these atomically, so a partial tool-call string can't be produced by accident — the chat template can rely on "if I see 163603, a tool_call_end follows at 163605 with well-formed JSON arguments between them". That's the concrete shape of the agentic data factory's "3000+ MCP tools" — every one of those tools is represented in training as a fragment that uses these specials, and the tokenizer treats them as first-class.

3. MuonClip in code — the exact post-update loop

No modeling file ships in the open-weights release, so the pseudocode in §muonclip is the best public reference. What you can see is how the attention block is structured for the clip to have a target: each MLA head exposes its own W_qc^h, W_kc^h, W_qr^h slices in memory (they're per-head sub-tensors inside W_q / W_k, not shared projection matrices). The DeepSeek-V3 modeling code on Hub (pinned at its own SHA for reference) is the cleanest place to read the per-head split: 

# MLA attention per-head split, paraphrased from DeepseekV3ForCausalLM
# modeling code at SHA a878eaff76… on Hub. K2 uses the same class.

# Q projection: 7168 -> q_lora_rank=1536 -> (num_heads, qk_nope_head_dim + qk_rope_head_dim)
q = self.q_a_proj(h)                            # [B, L, 1536]
q = self.q_a_layernorm(q)
q = self.q_b_proj(q)                            # [B, L, 64 * (128 + 64)]
q = q.view(B, L, 64, 128 + 64)                  # [B, L, num_heads, qk_head_dim]
q_c, q_r = q.split([128, 64], dim=-1)           # content / rope split per head

# K projection: shared kv_a + per-head kv_b, plus a separate k_rope from h
kv  = self.kv_a_proj_with_mqa(h)                # [B, L, 512 + 64]
c_kv, k_r = kv.split([512, 64], dim=-1)         # compressed latent + shared rotary
c_kv = self.kv_a_layernorm(c_kv)                # [B, L, 512]
kv  = self.kv_b_proj(c_kv)                      # [B, L, 64 * (128 + 128)]
kv  = kv.view(B, L, 64, 128 + 128)
k_c, v = kv.split([128, 128], dim=-1)           # per-head content key + value
k_r = k_r.view(B, L, 1, 64)                     # SHARED across heads

# MuonClip rescales W_qc^h / W_kc^h by sqrt(gamma_h), W_qr^h by gamma_h,
# NEVER kv_a_proj_with_mqa's k_rope channel (that's k_r, shared).

The shape comments are what matter: q_r and q_c are both per-head ([B, L, 64, ·]), but k_r is [B, L, 1, 64] — broadcast across heads at the attention score computation. That's exactly why MuonClip's §2.1 algorithm treats W_kr specially: the weight matrix that produced k_r is not per-head, so rescaling it would affect every head simultaneously. Without this asymmetry the per-head rescale wouldn't be well-defined, and the clip would have to become global. To avoid that, the rescale goes to the query side alone (W_qr^h gets the full gamma), while W_qc^h and W_kc^h both take the sqrt because both are per-head and both multiply into the pre-softmax logit.

4. checkpoint-engine — how 1T weights broadcast in 30s

The MoonshotAI/checkpoint-engine repo is the piece most likely to get adopted outside Moonshot — it's a drop-in "stream fresh policy weights from trainer to inferencer" service that keeps RL iterations from stalling on the weight transfer. The paper §3.3.2 claim that a full 1T-parameter refresh lands in under 30 seconds is load-bearing for the whole RL system: an RL iteration (rollout + update) typically takes minutes, so 30 seconds of weight-transfer becomes a fixed cost that stops scaling with model size.

Why the code walkthrough matters here — K2's paper is explicit about the algorithmic pieces (MuonClip, rubric-reward closed loop, partial rollout) but understated about the system-integration cost. Reading the shipped config + the checkpoint-engine repo together is the only way to see how cheap the composite was for Moonshot to pull off: the config is 90 lines of JSON and the weight-transfer service is a ~2K-line Python package. Everything else is the 15.5T-token pre-training run, which nobody else is re-running anyway.

takeaways // signed off

[01] The real contribution is post-training

MuonClip is beautiful engineering, but it's table stakes for anyone who wants Muon at 1T. K2's real moat is productionizing the agentic data factory and the closed-loop RL at 1T scale. Can others copy it? The data factory, sure (concept isn't hard). The closed-loop critic, sure (algorithmically simple). But running them jointly on a 1T model — 30-second checkpoint broadcast, 10k concurrent k8s sandboxes, partial rollout, one K2 serving as both actor and judge — that systems integration is what Moonshot is actually selling.

[02] Rubrics look like the next coordinate system after RLHF

Constitutional AI told us principles can stand in for human labels; K2 tells us task-specific rubrics can stand in for principles. All three point the same way: alignment signal moves from "human preference" → "global rules" → "task-specific checklists", each step more engineerable. I'd expect two or three more labs to ship rubric-reward post-training within two years.

[03] Non-thinking agents work fine

The entire paper is non-thinking. τ²-Bench 66.1 and SWE-Verified 65.8 are "reflex-grade" — which matters for agent latency. My prior was "agents need thinking"; K2 pushes back: when post-training bakes agentic patterns into the weights, inference-time thinking becomes optional. The cost doesn't disappear, it just shifts to the training end.

[04] A few open points I'd still verify

A few gaps the paper doesn't fill: RL τ (KL strength), rubric weighting, rollout size per RL iteration, agentic SFT token count, and the preference-data size for critic-head initialization. These are the biggest reproducibility gaps — "large-scale" without numbers. The activated params discrepancy (paper 32.6B vs HF 32B) I already flagged above.

[05] MuonClip was the symptom; Muon Split is the cause

The follow-up paper worth naming is GLM-5 (2026-02). Their Muon Split (see glm-5 deep-dive) replaces the per-head orthogonalization target: instead of feeding Muon's Newton-Schulz the whole [d_head · n_heads, d_latent] stacked matrix, split it into n_heads independent blocks and orthogonalize each separately. Each head gets its own singular-value budget; no head needs to grow weights to compensate; the logit blow-up MuonClip was invented to patch never happens. K2 treated the symptom with a post-update rescale; GLM-5 treats the cause with a smaller orthogonalization target. Read as a lineage: this is the paper that makes MuonClip a waypoint, not a destination.

[06] Closed-loop RL generalizes into cross-stage distillation

K2's trick is using verifiable rewards to anchor a subjective-reward critic. The Thinking Machines Lab on-policy-distillation piece reframes this axis as "sampling-policy × reward-density", and GLM-5's cross-stage distillation productionizes the on-policy half at stage-to-stage granularity. K2 closed the loop within one RL stage; GLM-5 closes it between stages. The map is the same — anchor a soft reward in something harder — but the scope is bigger. I'd read the three together.
