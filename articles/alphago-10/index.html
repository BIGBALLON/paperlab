AlphaGo at 10 · The Loop That Shed Its Priors

Ten years ago this spring, a program beat Lee Sedol 4–1 in Seoul. Two hundred million people watched. The DeepMind retrospective makes the anniversary case at the level of narrative — Move 37, the spark, the path to AGI. I want to make it at the level of mechanism. The 2016 headline was creativity. The 2026 headline, re-read with ten years of hindsight, is compression of priors. AlphaGo's real invention wasn't a neural network or a tree search — both existed — it was a closed loop in which policy, value, search, and self-play produce each other, and each loop around the track discards something the previous version required. Human games went first (Zero). Then domain engineering (AlphaZero). Then the rules themselves (MuZero). The system became more general by owning less.

TL;DR

AlphaGo (Silver et al., Nature 529, 28 Jan 2016) combined a supervised-learning policy network trained on ~30M expert moves, a reinforcement-learning policy refined by self-play, a value network trained to predict the self-play winner, and Monte-Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) guided by a PUCT selection rule (§Methods, Eqs. 1–3). The Lee Sedol match was played by the "AlphaGo Lee" build on 48 TPUs, Elo ≈ 3,739 (Wikipedia config table, citing Silver et al. Nature 2017). One loop turn later, AlphaGo Zero (Silver et al., Nature 550, 19 Oct 2017) dropped the SL corpus — self-play alone, merged policy+value into one tower, and exceeded AlphaGo Lee by Elo after ~3 days, reaching Elo 5,185 in 40 days on 4 TPUs. AlphaZero (Silver et al., arXiv:1712.01815, Dec 2017 → Science 362, 2018) dropped Go-specific input planes and symmetry augmentation; same algorithm mastered chess, shogi, and Go in 24 hours. MuZero (Schrittwieser et al., Nature 588, Dec 2020) dropped the rules themselves — search in a learned latent dynamics model, no simulator call inside the tree. The through-line is the loop shedding priors: human data → domain symmetries → environment rules → (today) natural-language verifier. AlphaProof + AlphaGeometry 2 reached IMO silver-medal level in July 2024; Gemini Deep Think hit gold at IMO 2025 using the same search-plus-reasoner architecture, graded in natural language. The 2016 magic trick was Move 37. The 2016–2026 mechanism was the loop, and what it has been shedding.

thesis // what was actually invented in 2016

Every retrospective on AlphaGo reaches for Move 37. Game 2 of the Lee Sedol match, a shoulder hit on the fifth line of the right side: commentators initially read it as a mistake, professional Go players around the room quietly revised their priors as the game continued, and a hundred moves later the stone was exactly where AlphaGo needed it. DeepMind's own retrospective reports that the policy network estimated that move's probability under expert play at ~1/10,000 — the canonical number the "creative AI" narrative has leaned on ever since.

That framing — a creative machine — is what made the 2016 news cycle. It is also what makes AlphaGo feel, ten years on, like a period piece: we moved on to language models, they are creative at volume, case closed. I think that reading misses the actual contribution, and misses why everything from OpenAI's o-series to DeepMind's AlphaEvolve to RLHF post-training descends from it.

claim

AlphaGo's contribution wasn't a neural network (a 13-layer convnet, 2016-grade), wasn't an SL corpus (KGS had existed for years), and wasn't MCTS (Crazy Stone and Zen already used it). The contribution was gluing them into a closed loop in which (i) the policy provides search priors, (ii) MCTS turns those priors into improved action distributions via planning, (iii) the improvements are distilled back into the policy, (iv) the value network scores leaves so you can stop rolling out, and (v) self-play generates the data all of this trains on. Every one of those arrows was separately known. Closing the loop — and showing that the loop iteratively improves — is the invention. The subsequent decade of DeepMind work is most cleanly read as which slot in this loop can be filled from less.

The rest of this piece takes that loop apart, names what each slot does, watches what each successor system removed from it, and argues that the systems that ate the world after 2020 — ChatGPT-style RLHF, o1-style inference-time search, agentic math provers, evolutionary code search — all run this loop with different priors in different slots. Sources: I read the three Nature papers (2016, 2017, 2020) and the AlphaZero arXiv end-to-end; pulled move counts and game dates from the Wikipedia article for cross-checks; confirmed Elo and hardware from its configuration table, which itself cites Silver et al. 2017. Where a number is paper-reported, blog-reported, my arithmetic, or unverifiable, I tag it.

the loop // four networks, one tree, one tape of self-play

The 2016 system has four trained components and one non-trained one (Silver et al. 2016, Fig. 1). Read the figure left-to-right for training, bottom for search: a supervised policy $p_\sigma$ (13-layer CNN, 57.0% top-1 on ~30M KGS 6–9 dan moves) and a fast hand-crafted rollout $p_\pi$ (~1000× faster) are both fitted off the human corpus; an RL policy $p_\rho$ is initialized from $p_\sigma$ and refined by REINFORCE on $\pm 1$ self-play outcomes; the self-play games become a 30M-position tape (one position per game, to decorrelate) that trains the value network $v_\theta$; at inference a single MCTS composes $p_\sigma$ as tree prior, $v_\theta$ as leaf evaluator, and $p_\pi$ as rollout, picks the most-visited root child, and played games feed back into the tape.

Read the top row as the training dependency chain (corpus → SL → RL → tape → value, with the fast rollout as a parallel branch off the corpus), and the bottom box as the single per-move MCTS that composes the three of them (prior P from $p_\sigma$, leaf V blended from $v_\theta$ and rollout $z$, choose by visit count). The dashed pink feedback arrow is what makes it a loop rather than a pipeline: played games grow the tape, which retrains the value. The coloured mapping (cyan SL, violet RL, green value/search, amber hand-crafted, pink self-play) is reused across the rest of the article — in the shedding table (§shedding) each removed prior corresponds to one of these colours going away. Source: Silver et al. Nature 529 (2016), Fig. 1 & §Methods.

Three facts about this layout matter more than they get credit for. First, the four networks are not a stack — they don't compose in series on inference. They compose through the tree: $p_\sigma$ shapes its breadth, $v_\theta$ shapes its depth termination, $p_\pi$ fills the gap between the two. A 2016-grade 13-layer CNN was not enough to pick a move by itself (the raw policy was beaten by many amateur dans); the tree was what turned a good suggestion machine into a superhuman deciding machine. Second, the dataset the value network trains on is generated by a different network ($p_\rho$) playing against itself. That is the first appearance of the self-play tape as an explicit data source in a system that actually worked at scale. Third, the policy whose distribution the tree uses ($p_\sigma$) and the policy that generated the training data for the scoring function ($p_\rho$) are distinct. AlphaGo 2016 has two policies in the system, one for exploration breadth and one for data generation. The successor versions quietly collapse this back into one — and removing that redundancy is exactly what lets them drop the SL corpus.

puct // the one formula that decides what AlphaGo thinks about

MCTS is a family. The specific instantiation AlphaGo uses — inherited from Rémi Coulom's 2006 Crazy Stone and Gelly & Silver (2007)'s UCT-with-priors — selects an action at each in-tree node by the PUCT rule. The 2016 version (Silver et al. 2016, §Methods, Eq. 1):

$$a_t \;=\; \arg\max_a \Big[\;\underbrace{Q(s_t, a)}_{\text{exploit}} \;+\; \underbrace{u(s_t, a)}_{\text{explore}}\;\Big], \qquad u(s, a) \;=\; c_{\text{puct}}\cdot P(a\mid s)\cdot \frac{\sqrt{\sum_b N(s, b)}}{1 + N(s, a)}$$

Four symbols, and each is a decision about which prior the tree trusts. $Q(s,a)$ is the average leaf value from simulations that passed through edge $(s,a)$ — the empirical prior, built by the tree itself as it runs. $P(a\mid s)$ is the SL policy's probability for action $a$ — the human prior, loaded from a neural network evaluated once per node. $N(s,a)$ is the visit count — the attention budget already spent on that edge. $c_{\text{puct}}$ is a scalar the authors set — the only tunable knob in the formula. The tree works by deflating the neural prior as visits accumulate: when $N(s,a)$ is small, $u$ is dominated by $c_{\text{puct}}\cdot P(a\mid s)$ and the network decides; when $N(s,a)$ is large, $u$ shrinks and $Q$ dominates. In-tree, priors decay.

The leaf evaluation is the other half. On reaching a leaf $s_L$, AlphaGo computes a blend of the value network and the fast rollout (Silver et al. 2016, Eq. 2):

$$V(s_L) \;=\; (1 - \lambda)\, v_\theta(s_L) \;+\; \lambda\, z(s_L), \qquad \lambda = 0.5 \text{ in the match configuration.}$$

where $z(s_L)$ is the outcome of a single rollout to terminal under the fast policy $p_\pi$. Both estimators are biased in different ways — the value network is biased toward the distribution it was trained on (self-play under $p_\rho$), the rollout is biased toward positions the fast policy handles well — and the mix hedges. The ablation (Silver et al. 2016, Fig. 4) shows each estimator alone is weaker than the blend; $\lambda=0.5$ is the sweet spot, and it's one of the few places where the paper admits the system still leans on hand-craft.

drift — what Zero changed in PUCT

AlphaGo Zero keeps the same PUCT form but drops the rollout term entirely: $V(s_L) = v_\theta(s_L)$, $\lambda = 0$ (Silver et al. 2017, §Methods). No fast rollout policy exists; the linear model with hand-crafted features is gone. This matters because the rollout was the most hand-engineered piece of AlphaGo Lee — dozens of pattern-match features, a whole parallel training pipeline — and it was load-bearing at $\lambda=0.5$. Zero removes it on the claim that the value network, trained on a much larger and more diverse self-play tape, is accurate enough to carry the full evaluation. The empirical evidence: Zero's Elo climbs past AlphaGo Lee in 3 days and past AlphaGo Master in 21, on 4 TPUs (Silver et al. 2017, Fig. 3). That is the strongest piece of evidence that hand-engineered rollout features, which cost Lee's team months, were not load-bearing in principle — they were load-bearing relative to the value network's training distribution at that scale. Shrink the hand-craft, widen the data, the value network catches up.

The reason PUCT is the most reusable idea in the 2016 paper has little to do with Go. It is a principled way of spending a fixed compute budget between a learned prior you trust up to a point and an empirical estimate you are accumulating by thinking. Every current "inference-time compute" system is doing some version of this — Tree-of-Thought, o1's hidden-CoT sampling, AlphaEvolve's population search over LLM-proposed code edits, AlphaProof's search over Lean proof tactics. The prior is an LLM; the empirical estimate is a verifier (unit tests, theorem-prover assent, graded rubric); PUCT-shaped selection rules decide where to spend the next thousand forward passes. AlphaGo's Eq. 1, stripped of its $19\times19$ board, is the central equation of a decade of agentic search.

the self-improvement equation — why the loop actually closes

PUCT explains how AlphaGo thinks. It does not explain why the system gets stronger over time. For that, Silver et al. 2017 (Zero) states the cleanest equation in the whole line. Merge the policy and value heads into one residual tower $f_\theta(s) = (\mathbf{p}_\theta(\cdot|s),\; v_\theta(s))$, and train it on self-play games where each position $s$ carries two targets: the MCTS visit distribution $\boldsymbol{\pi}$ and the game outcome $z\in\{-1, +1\}$.

$$L(\theta) \;=\; \underbrace{(z - v_\theta(s))^2}_{\text{value MSE}} \;-\; \underbrace{\boldsymbol{\pi}^\top \log \mathbf{p}_\theta(\cdot|s)}_{\text{policy cross-entropy}} \;+\; \underbrace{c\,\|\theta\|_2^2}_{\text{L2 reg}}.$$

Three terms. One tower. No SL corpus, no rollout, no separate RL loop — every component of the four-network 2016 system has been absorbed into two heads over a shared trunk. The line to stare at is the middle one. The policy head is trained to predict what the tree decided, not what a human did. And what the tree decided is the tempered visit distribution:

$$\boldsymbol{\pi}(a \mid s) \;=\; \frac{N(s, a)^{1/\tau}}{\sum_b N(s, b)^{1/\tau}}, \qquad \tau \to 0 \text{ after the opening moves.}$$

This is the pivot the rest of the decade is built on. MCTS is not just a decision-time wrapper; it's a policy-improvement operator $\mathcal{T}$ that takes a prior $\mathbf{p}_\theta$ and a value $v_\theta$ and returns a new distribution $\boldsymbol{\pi} = \mathcal{T}(\mathbf{p}_\theta, v_\theta)$ that is provably stronger than $\mathbf{p}_\theta$ when $v_\theta$ is a reasonable estimator (Grill et al. 2020 later formalized this as a regularized-policy-iteration step; the intuition is older and goes back to Gelly & Silver 2007). Once you see MCTS this way, the closed loop has a clean mathematical shape:

the loop as regularized policy iteration

(i) improve: $\boldsymbol{\pi} = \mathcal{T}(\mathbf{p}_\theta, v_\theta)$ — spend search budget, produce stronger per-state distribution.
(ii) evaluate: play the game to terminal under $\boldsymbol{\pi}$, observe outcome $z$.
(iii) distill: minimize $L(\theta)$ to pull $\mathbf{p}_\theta$ toward $\boldsymbol{\pi}$ and $v_\theta$ toward $z$.
(iv) repeat. Stronger $\mathbf{p}_\theta$ makes MCTS spend its visits smarter; stronger $v_\theta$ makes the leaves reliable enough to shrink rollouts. Each round of the loop tightens the next.

The reason this matters beyond Go: this is the exact same shape used by every post-2023 LLM RL recipe that produces strong reasoners. Replace MCTS with "sample $K$ CoT completions and keep the top-$k$ by verifier score"; replace $\boldsymbol{\pi}$ with the empirical distribution of kept completions; replace the cross-entropy term with a KL or a clipped-ratio objective, and you have DeepSeek-Math's GRPO, DeepSeek-R1-Zero's outer loop, or the Kimi-K2 self-critique rubric loop. The policy-improvement operator slot — once occupied by MCTS over a board, now occupied by rejection sampling over a reasoning chain — is the thing doing the actual work. What changed in ten years isn't that we stopped using AlphaGo Zero's equation. It's that we got less picky about what counts as a search.

37 and 78 // one move each direction through the 1-in-10,000 door

Any honest write-up has to spend a section on the two moves, because the symmetry is the lesson. Both Move 37 (AlphaGo, Game 2, White) and Move 78 (Lee Sedol, Game 4, White) were reported, after the fact, to have roughly 1/10,000 probability under their respective policies. That they are cited identically is not a coincidence — and the asymmetry in how each was produced is exactly the gap between 2016 AI and a world Go champion.

The two board diagrams below are schematic, not move-by-move reconstructions — they render the type of move (shoulder hit on the fifth line; wedge into a contact fight) at approximate coordinates. Full SGF records are linked from the AlphaGo vs Lee Sedol Wikipedia article.

Game 2 · AlphaGo (White) · Move 37

AlphaGo's white shoulder-hit on the fifth line of the right side, Game 2. Amateur instinct is that moves on the fifth line away from a framework are overreach — the conservative wisdom plays on the fourth. AlphaGo's policy $p_\sigma$ gave this move a prior of ~1/10,000 under expert play (DeepMind, AlphaGo page); the tree promoted it anyway because $v_\theta$ estimated a higher win-rate along that line than the popular alternatives, and the visits along that edge validated the value estimate as the simulations deepened. In PUCT terms: $P$ was tiny, but $Q$ caught up before $u$ decayed.

Game 4 · Lee Sedol (White) · Move 78

Lee Sedol's white wedge, Game 4. Aja Huang confirmed in a June 2016 Netherlands presentation that AlphaGo's value network did not anticipate this move, and its value estimate failed to adjust smoothly after it was played; AlphaGo then produced a sequence of weak moves and Lee won by resignation at move 180. This is the only game AlphaGo lost in its 74 official matches (Wikipedia AlphaGo, §Match against Lee Sedol, citing news coverage 2018). The lesson that ran from humans to DeepMind: neural value estimators on rare distributions are brittle; self-play's coverage was incomplete.

Read these two moves as a pair and they make one point: the prior distribution you sampled from decides which creative moves cost you thinking time. Move 37 was 1/10,000 under $p_\sigma$ — a human-trained prior. The tree promoted it to a decision because $v_\theta$ (trained on $p_\rho$-self-play, not on human games) disagreed with the human prior. Move 78 was 1/10,000 under $v_\theta$'s own distribution — the self-play tape had not visited positions shaped like that wedge, so the leaf value estimates along it were unreliable, and the PUCT formula's $Q$ term didn't stabilize. What broke AlphaGo in Game 4 was not a lack of creativity but a coverage gap in the self-play distribution. Silver et al. 2017 (Zero) doesn't name Move 78, but the architectural move — throw away the SL corpus, run self-play far wider — is exactly the fix that coverage gap asked for.

The pattern is larger than Go. Every system that combines a learned prior with a search or verifier has a "Move 78 problem": failures cluster in the region of input space the generator's self-play has not covered, and a verifier trained on that self-play cannot be trusted there. Modern RL post-training has a name for this — distribution shift under the target policy — and most of the solutions that work (on-policy rollouts, reward model re-training, on-policy distillation) are at heart the same move AlphaGo Zero makes: regenerate the data under the current distribution, retrain the verifier on that data. Lee Sedol's divine move is not a footnote. It's the first clean instance of the brittleness that the next decade of alignment work has been paying for.

the Bitter Lesson, with receipts

Rich Sutton's 2019 essay argued that the methods which have won, historically, are the ones that scale with compute and lean on general-purpose learning and search, not hand-crafted domain knowledge. AlphaGo's shedding sequence is the cleanest single case-study for that claim. Fan's system had 48 Go-specific feature planes + rollout patterns + an SL corpus; Lee's TPU version shrank the hardware but kept the features; Master's briefing at Wuzhen was explicitly "we removed most of the hand-crafted features"; Zero removed the last of them plus the SL corpus; AlphaZero removed Go-specific input planes and symmetry augmentation; MuZero removed the rules. Every decrement improved the system. The hand-crafted pieces that felt load-bearing at each step turned out not to be, once the general learning signal was scaled. AlphaGo is the paper of record for why the bitter lesson is bitter — people worked for years on rollout features and feature-engineered input planes that the next version cut to zero lines of code with no performance loss. If you're writing domain knowledge into a model today and the verifier is cheap, a sceptical reading is: this is what Zero dropped.

What each removal bought

The seductive reading of this table is "every system is a scaled-up version of the previous one, and the field is just scaling". That is not what the papers say. Every removal is a negative result that made a positive system possible. Let me take them one at a time.

[Zero ↩ SL] data you didn't have wasn't data you needed

The 2017 paper's cleanest claim is that starting from random and running self-play surpasses starting from KGS + self-play, given enough training. Three days to beat AlphaGo Lee, 21 days to beat AlphaGo Master, 40 days to beat all previous versions. This is the strongest evidence we have that an expensive human-data curation step can be completely redundant when the search signal is accurate and cheap. Every RLHF follow-up that has run a pure-RL ablation (DeepSeek-Math's GRPO work, DeepSeek-R1-Zero) has hit a version of this result.

[AlphaZero ↩ Go features] the recipe wasn't Go-specific

AlphaGo Lee's input was 48 feature planes: liberties, turns since last move, ladder-captured groups, ladder-escape, sensibleness, etc. — all Go-specific. AlphaZero cuts this down to 17 planes (8 history pairs + player-to-move; Silver et al. 2017b, Tab. S1): nothing Go-specific except the board. The data augmentation by the 8 symmetries of the square is also cut, because chess isn't rotation-invariant and the authors wanted one algorithm across games. What's left is a template: policy+value tower, PUCT, self-play, Dirichlet-noise at the root for exploration. The 24-hour training run across three games is a deliberate demonstration: the template generalizes.

[MuZero ↩ rules] even the simulator wasn't load-bearing

AlphaZero's MCTS calls the true game simulator at every in-tree step to compute the next state. That is free in Go/chess/shogi — but it's a fiction in the real world. MuZero replaces the simulator with three learned functions: $h_\theta$ (representation: observation → latent), $g_\theta$ (dynamics: (latent, action) → next latent + reward), $f_\theta$ (prediction: latent → policy + value). Search runs entirely inside the latent model. The striking empirical fact is that this doesn't lose strength in Go/chess/shogi and becomes SoTA on Atari — where no exact simulator is available. The conceptual fact is bigger: the structure that made AlphaZero work was search+prediction in a well-calibrated latent world model, not search in the real world. Every current "world model for RL" line of work descends from this.

[AlphaProof ↩ numerical verifier] the rule slot can be natural-language

The 2024 AlphaProof/AlphaGeometry 2 system that reached silver-medal IMO standard is, architecturally, AlphaZero with Lean 4 as the environment: a Gemini-finetuned policy proposes tactics, Lean's kernel verifies, self-play over a fixed problem distribution generates the tape. Three of the four solved problems were solved by this loop; the geometry problem by AlphaGeometry 2. The 2025 version reached gold-medal level using Gemini Deep Think, where the verifier is a graded-rubric language model and the search is over chains of natural-language reasoning. The loop's "rule" slot is filled by whatever is cheap and correct on the domain. In math, that's Lean. In code, it's unit tests. In open-ended reasoning, it's graded language. The slot is domain-agnostic.

The shedding sequence is not compression for its own sake. It is the map of which priors were fake. Each successor system ran a controlled experiment on the last one: remove the SL corpus, does the loop still close? Remove the Go symmetries, does it still close? Remove the simulator, does it still close? Replace the numerical verifier with a linguistic one, does it still close? The answer has been yes, four times in a row. What that tells you is that the loop itself — policy-proposes, verifier-scores, search-improves, distill-back — is the part doing the work. The priors were scaffolding around a real core. It is, unusually for AI, a decade of scientific negative results, and the field's understanding of what's actually load-bearing is better because of them.

compute-inversion // where the search budget lives has flipped

There is an axis I did not see named in any of the AlphaGo retrospectives that I think cuts cleanly: when, in the system's lifecycle, is the search performed? AlphaGo and o-series answer that question oppositely, and a lot of the apparent weirdness of 2026 post-training vanishes if you read it through this axis.

AlphaGo Zero. At training time, every single self-play move is an MCTS with 1,600 simulations (Silver et al. 2017, §Methods, "each move used ~1,600 simulations, ~0.4 s thinking time per move"). A full game is ~200 moves, so one self-play game consumes roughly $1{,}600 \times 200 = 320{,}000$ evaluations of the policy-value tower — and the full 40-day training run stacks millions of such games. The final weight file is a distillation of all that search into a pair of heads. At deployment, MCTS is still used — but Fig. 4a of the same paper plots the "raw network" strength (policy head alone, no search) climbing well above AlphaGo Lee's Elo gridline (3,739) by training end, while the full MCTS version adds another ~1,500–2,000 Elo on top. The 40-day training run shifted the bulk of the compute budget into the weights; inference-time search became a multiplier, not a crutch.

o-series / Deep Think / AlphaEvolve. At training time, search is usually absent or nearly so — pre-training is next-token prediction, post-training is RLHF against a reward model on short rollouts. At inference, search is where the compute lives: sample $K$ chains, keep the ones a verifier accepts, optionally grow a tree over partial chains. Deep Think's gold-medal IMO 2025 run was hours per problem; AlphaEvolve spends a population-search budget per algorithm discovery. The per-query compute is elastic and enormous.

the dual use of search

Search-at-train-time and search-at-test-time are duals. Both are ways to extract more value from a weak policy via a verifier. The choice between them is dictated by (i) how narrow the task distribution is — narrow tasks can amortize a big train-time search budget across many deployments, (ii) how cheap the verifier is — cheap verifiers make train-time search scalable, (iii) how elastic the test-time compute is — open-ended queries can pay per-query. Go has a narrow distribution, free verifier, and rigid test-time compute. General reasoning has a wide distribution, expensive verifier, and elastic test-time compute. The two systems make the opposite choice and each choice is correct for its setting. The interesting frontier is the ratio: how much of your total compute do you spend on train-time distillation vs test-time sampling? Nobody has a principled answer yet, and I'd bet the next generation of RL post-training work is largely an experiment on that ratio.

Read the last five years of "reasoning" papers through the compute-inversion axis and most of the confusing differences go away. On-Policy Distillation sits close to AlphaGo Zero — it spends search-like sampling per training step and distills into the policy, then deploys cheaply. Deep Think sits at the far other end — train the policy on verifiable rewards without search, spend all search at inference. GRPO, R1-Zero, and most RLHF recipes sit somewhere in the middle, doing some train-time rejection sampling but much less than 1,600-sim-per-move. The question "is AlphaGo's playbook dead or alive in 2026?" is malformed. AlphaGo's playbook is the playbook; we're just running it with different values of where the search budget lives.

the echo // where AlphaGo's loop is running right now

If the loop — policy + search + verifier + self-play tape + distillation — is the real contribution, you should expect to see it rattling around in 2026-era frontier systems that have nothing visible to do with Go. I claim you do. Here are four places I see it clearly, and one place I think it's missing.

[echo 1] o-series / Deep Think

Inference-time search over reasoning chains. The observable behaviour: spend more wall-clock per query, get a better answer, with scaling holding cleanly. Mechanically: sample $K$ candidate chains from a policy LLM, score each with a verifier (rubric, self-consistency, or internal reward model), pick the best — or, in the tree variant, grow a PUCT-shaped tree over partial chains. The 2016 AlphaGo equation is doing exactly this; the LLM is $p_\sigma$, the graded rubric is $v_\theta$, Eq. 1 decides which branch to spend the next sample on. Gemini Deep Think's IMO 2025 gold is the direct descendant.

[echo 2] RLHF post-training

The reward model is the value network. An LLM policy gets fine-tuned against a learned scorer that rates completions, and the policy's training corpus is its own samples. Policy + value, self-play tape, distill. The 2016 loop, at scale, with language instead of Go positions. The brittleness point is the same: reward models overfit to the generator they were trained on, and systems drift (the familiar "over-optimization" failure). The fix has looked a lot like "rerun self-play under the current policy, retrain the reward model" — AlphaGo Zero's mitigation, at different tokens.

[echo 3] AlphaProof · AlphaEvolve

The same template with different verifiers. AlphaProof uses Lean kernel assent; AlphaEvolve uses program-score on held-out tasks; AlphaChip uses PPA-metric rewards on chip floorplans. Each is the AlphaGo loop with one component replaced: the environment / verifier. DeepMind has been running the same playbook — find a domain with a cheap correctness signal, instantiate the loop — for eight years. Most of Demis Hassabis's retrospective is a reading of which domains had the right kind of verifier.

[echo 4] World-model RL

MuZero's latent dynamics, everywhere. Dreamer-V3, Genie, LeCun-lineage JEPA world models, Sora-flavoured video-next-frame objectives, Nvidia's Cosmos. The shape — learn a latent dynamics model cheap to roll out in, plan inside it — is Zero-to-MuZero's contribution transplanted to domains where the real simulator is unaffordable or nonexistent. LeWorldModel is the most recent clean instance; it swaps MuZero's prediction head for SIGReg and trains on a single L40S, but the plan-in-latent-world-model shape is 2020-MuZero.

echo in public — KataGo and the open-source line

The AlphaGo / AlphaZero papers ship no code and no weights. The open-source lineage that actually carries the loop to practitioners — and extends it — is KataGo (Wu, 2019), alongside Facebook's ELF OpenGo and Leela Zero. KataGo is worth calling out specifically because it isn't just an AlphaZero re-implementation: Wu's paper contributes three knobs that are now standard in any "search + self-play" system, and all three exist because plain AlphaZero was wasteful in ways the original papers don't discuss. (i) Playout-cap randomization — most self-play moves get a small budget (fast exploration); a few get a large budget with full training targets (careful distillation). (ii) Auxiliary prediction targets — besides $(\boldsymbol{\pi}, z)$, the network predicts territory ownership, final score, game length. These aren't used at inference; they're regularizers that force the trunk to learn features it would otherwise miss. (iii) Score maximization rather than win-probability, so the engine plays better under handicap and analysis. The shape generalizes: cheap exploration dense, expensive distillation sparse is a pattern I'd bet reappears in serious LLM RL stacks this year. If you want to actually read a self-play-plus-search codebase end-to-end, KataGo is the 10k-line cousin of MuZero with the engineering lessons written in.

the missing echo — open-ended self-play

The part of AlphaGo that hasn't transplanted cleanly is true open-ended self-play. In Go, the self-play tape worked because the environment had a fixed payoff (win/loss) and a fixed state space; two copies of the policy playing each other kept improving via a well-defined zero-sum gradient. For language, code, math, there is no canonical adversary, no zero-sum structure, and "self-play" has meant "one copy of the model generates, another copy judges" — which is much closer to distillation than to Lee Sedol-vs-AlphaGo. The systems that have gotten closest — AlphaProof's problem-pool approach, debate-style self-critique — have a problem distribution the self-play walks over, not an adversary it competes against. The missing piece is the part of AlphaGo that was most visibly magical: two copies of the system playing against each other invented new strategies the humans hadn't thought of. We have not reproduced that in any post-2022 domain, and I think it's the open question that most cleanly divides what's left of AlphaGo's legacy from the world models + RLHF that consumed it.

still open // what ten years has not given us

The retrospective temptation is to read the decade as a clean climb. It isn't. Here are four AlphaGo-style problems that are still unsolved in 2026, and that I'd argue are what the next ten years of this line actually has to produce.

[A] Move 78 at scale — KataGo, ~97% beatable in 2022

The cleanest scaled-up confirmation of the Move-78 distribution-gap thesis is Wang, Gleave et al. 2022, Adversarial Policies Beat Superhuman Go AIs. They trained an adversarial policy against KataGo — a system stronger than any human, with six years of compounding self-play since AlphaGo Zero — and achieved a >97% win rate against KataGo at the setting used by pro commentators. The attack is not subtle: the adversary plays "blatantly bad moves" an amateur 5-kyu would punish, but exploits a narrow hole in KataGo's distribution by building a cyclic group that the value head consistently mis-evaluates. Subsequent work (Tseng et al. 2023) extended this to multiple KataGo builds. The diagnosis: six years of self-play did not close the distribution gap that Lee Sedol punched through in Game 4. It just moved it somewhere humans weren't looking. Every claim that a self-play-trained system is "robust" should be read against this result — the Move-78 problem is not historical, it is still the state of the art, and it is the reason alignment people lose sleep.

[A′] verifier collapse in open domains

AlphaGo Zero works because Go has a cheap, correct verifier: the game rules. AlphaProof inherits Lean's kernel. AlphaEvolve has unit tests. The minute the verifier is itself a learned model (rubric, reward model, graded rubric), the loop develops a reward-hacking failure mode that the cheap-verifier systems don't have. Hassabis's retrospective is honest about this — "open-ended" is the word he uses for where Deep Think is extending — but the verifier-collapse problem does not yet have a crisp solution, and the 2024–2026 RLHF literature is mostly a catalogue of partial mitigations.

[B] transfer across domains

AlphaZero's chess-shogi-Go generality is very narrow: three perfect-information 2-player zero-sum games. MuZero extends this to one-player stochastic games (Atari), which is still narrow. There is no single learned loop that moves from a finished game of Go to a half-built code repository to a proof of a new theorem — the template transfers, but the trained system does not. The analogue to AlphaFold's "200M structures for free, one network" would be "any verifier-equipped domain, one trained loop", and that does not exist.

[C] inventing the game

Hassabis's closing line in the 10-year retrospective is that the real test of AGI creativity would be not Move 37 — a move within Go — but inventing a game as deep and elegant as Go. I think this is an underrated framing. Every AlphaGo-style system picks up a pre-specified game or proof-system and plays better than humans inside it. None of them has produced an object — a game, a theory, a mathematical structure — that other researchers then want to study as an object in its own right. The loop optimizes inside a frame; it doesn't produce the frame.

[D] the compute-per-insight curve bent the wrong way

AlphaGo Fan to AlphaGo Zero is a compression: Elo went up, hardware went down (1,202 CPUs + 176 GPUs → 4 TPUs). That trajectory has not continued. Post-2020 frontier systems are spending orders of magnitude more compute per task than AlphaZero did, and delivering, on per-task reasoning quality, wins that are large but not orders-of-magnitude large. The decade's compression story — do more with less — reversed sometime around 2021. Whether that's because the loop genuinely needs more compute at language scale, or because we stopped asking the "can we remove this?" question the way the 2017–2020 DeepMind papers did, is the tension worth watching.

takeaways // signed off

[01] The loop is the invention

Policy proposes, verifier scores, search improves, distillation closes. Deep neural networks existed before AlphaGo. MCTS existed before AlphaGo. Self-play existed before AlphaGo. Gluing them into a loop that iteratively improves did not. That is the primitive the decade since has been reusing, with different components in different slots.

[02] Removals are the lesson, not the additions

AlphaGo Fan → Lee → Master → Zero → AlphaZero → MuZero is a sequence of subtractions: human data, hand-crafted features, Go-specific features, even the simulator. Each subtraction was a claim that a piece everyone thought was load-bearing was not. Reading AI progress as "more of the same, scaled up" misses this. The cleanest progress — including in 2026 — has come from figuring out which inputs you can drop.

[03] Move 78 was the grown-up result

Move 37 is the quotable moment. Move 78 — Lee Sedol's wedge that AlphaGo couldn't process — is the one that actually shaped research. It diagnosed distribution-shift brittleness under the generator's own policy, and the fix for it (retrain the verifier on fresh on-policy data) is the single most reused pattern of the RLHF era. Almost every alignment failure mode that has a name now was foreshadowed in that game.

[04] The verifier slot is where progress happens next

Every echo of the AlphaGo loop in the real world — AlphaProof in Lean, AlphaEvolve in code, Deep Think in natural-language proofs — is defined by what goes in the verifier slot. Domains with cheap, correct verifiers (formal proofs, code with tests, games with rules) have made clean progress. Domains without them (open-ended dialog, creative writing, science taste) have not. The bottleneck on applying the loop further isn't policy quality or search algorithm — those are commodity now — it's the verifier. Ten years on, the AlphaGo paper's most underrated line is still Eq. 1, and the question it raises is where's your verifier?

AlphaGo's legacy is not that machines can be creative, or that they can beat world champions, or that Go turned out to be computable. It is that a particular loop — a learned policy seeded from some prior, refined by search against a verifier, evaluated by a network trained on its own samples — is the scaffolding of a general optimization engine, and that the scaffolding survives every removal of its domain-specific parts. In 2016 the scaffolding was holding up a Go-playing program. In 2026 it is holding up theorem provers, coding agents, robot controllers, and the better of the two flavours of LLM post-training. Ten years from now, when we look back at 2026-era systems, we'll say the same thing: the surface was the output, the mechanism was the loop. Who filled which slot with what has been the real story all along.
