Kimi K2.5 · Dip-and-Recover: The Real Cost of Bolting Vision onto a 1T MoE

The paper sat on my desk for three days and I wore out the moonshotai/Kimi-K2.5 HF repo, so I sat down and pulled the whole thing apart — from Kimi K2's 1T text-only MoE base, through ~15 T multimodal tokens of continual pretraining, all the way down to the three post-training stages: Zero-Vision SFT → Joint RL → PARL — at layer-and-field granularity. This long read is my own reading log. I'll walk you through it the way I'd walk a teammate through a debug trace: stop at every counter-intuitive choice, ask why they did it, what the code actually does, and how much it's really worth.

overview // what Kimi K2.5 actually is

Let me get the skeleton out of the way: Kimi K2.5 is not trained from scratch as a multimodal model. It starts from Moonshot's own 1T MoE Kimi K2, does continual pretraining on ~15 T multimodal tokens — growing vision into the "text skeleton" — and then caps it off with three post-training passes: Zero-Vision SFT → Joint Multimodal RL → PARL.

My read, after sitting with the paper for a few days: text and vision aren't two tracks trading off against each other, they're the same track buffing each other up — an idea I come back to in every section below. The bit I love is how many load-bearing design choices fall out of that one frame once you accept it.

three core ideas

  • Native Multimodal Pre-training — Early Fusion + a flat ~10% low-vision ratio
  • Zero-Vision SFT — text-only SFT that activates visual reasoning & tool calls
  • Agent Swarm + PARL — trainable Orchestrator + frozen Sub-agents, RL'd in parallel

unified across modality / mode / form

  • Vision + Language: images, video, OCR, GUI, charts — one pipeline
  • Thinking + Instant: two inference modes, thinking togglable
  • Chat + Agent: dialog, tool orchestration and agent swarm in one model

headline numbers

  • HLE-Full (w/ tools) 50.2 — beats Gemini 3 Pro (45.8) & GPT-5.2 (45.5)
  • BrowseComp (Swarm) 78.4 — beats GPT-5.2 (65.8)
  • LongVideoBench 79.8 / LVBench 75.9 — long-video SOTA
  • OCRBench 92.3 / OmniDocBench 1.5 88.8

the two threads I see running through it

early fusion pretrain // dissecting the mm pre-training

This is the section I most want you to get. My first reaction was to scroll right past the ablation table, because "vision mix ratio" sounds like the kind of knob you tune in week 2. It isn't: on a fixed vision+text budget, how you mix vision data matters far more than how much you mix. Let me put the question I had at the time on the page first —

the question I was sitting with

what I (and most people) defaulted to — Qwen-VL / InternVL style
Pretrain a big LLM on pure text until the text ability stabilizes, then inject a bunch of vision tokens late in the run — typically at a ≥ 50% vision ratio. Multimodal as a "visual patch" slapped onto the language model. This used to be my default too.
Kimi K2.5's counter-answer
On a fixed vision+text budget, Early Fusion + a flat ~10% low-vision ratio wins on both the vision and text sides simultaneously. The first time I saw this ablation, one thought stuck in my head: oh, this is how you mix.

the "Dip-and-Recover" effect (Figure 9 + Appendix B.1)
Observed under mid/late fusion: the moment vision data is introduced, text ability dips first, then recovers. The paper attributes this to "modality domain shift" — abruptly injecting vision tokens shocks the existing language representation space, and the model has to sacrifice text performance temporarily to cross-align. Early Fusion mixes vision in at a low ratio from step zero, so the gradient landscape stays smooth and there's no representation collapse.

zero-vision sft // activating vision without seeing an image

I spent a long time on this one. A pretrained VLM does not automatically learn "when you see an image, crop and OCR first" — that's the classic cold-start problem for multimodal RL. I was surprised by the answer: I fully expected Moonshot to write reams of hand-crafted visual CoT; instead the move in this section is no vision SFT data at all — the SFT stage is fed pure text only.

It sounds like sorcery, but once I walked the code path I got it — Stage-2's Early Fusion has already welded vision into the text skeleton, so SFT's only job is teaching the model when to reach for IPython. Exactly how the code reads pixels or counts objects is prior knowledge the vision-text pair already shares; no need to re-feed it.

the old playbook's ceiling

  • visual CoT either hand-annotated or prompt-synthesized
  • tool actions locked to primitives like crop / rotate / flip
  • low data diversity, rigid reasoning patterns
  • easy to overfit to the annotation distribution

zero-vision sft, in pieces

  • SFT stage uses no image SFT data whatsoever — high-quality text SFT only
  • every visual operation is programmatic via IPython — equivalent to arbitrary Python
  • what emerges on its own: binarization + counting to gauge object size, pixel-level ops, localization, OCR, counting
  • the prerequisite for any of this: Stage-2 Joint Pretrain must have built a strong enough vision-text alignment
the counter-intuitive finding (paper §2.2)
Their ablation shows: adding hand-designed visual SFT traces actually hurts visual generalization. Likely reason: Stage-2 has already locked in the alignment, and hand-crafted visual SFT is too narrow in quality/diversity — it can only add bias.

joint multimodal rl // vision RL that makes text better

I went with "the RL stage is mostly vision polish" as my mental model going in. Then the table showing text benchmarks going up after vision RL stopped me cold — MMLU-Pro +1.7, GPQA +2.1, LongBench v2 +2.2. No text training, and text got stronger. My take is that the ability-partitioned reward (below) is doing most of the work here — the modality label is almost a distraction. This section is my attempt to unpack why.

why does vision RL strengthen text?
The paper's take: vision RL improves calibration of structured information extraction, which reduces uncertainty for "visually-grounded-style" text queries (counting, OCR-like extraction), giving a two-way transfer. They call this bidirectional enhancement — text bootstraps vision, vision refines text.

partition by ability, not by modality

ability-based domains instead of modality-based
Classic multimodal training splits domains into "vision expert / text expert." K2.5 flips it: RL domains are knowledge / reasoning / coding / agentic, and each domain expert consumes both pure-text and multimodal queries; the GRM rates across modalities uniformly. The win: capability gains transfer bidirectionally by construction, maximizing cross-modal transfer.

policy optimization

$$ \mathcal{L}_{RL}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{x\sim D}\Bigg[\frac{1}{N}\sum_{j=1}^{K}\sum_{i=1}^{|y_j|}\underbrace{\mathrm{Clip}\!\left(\frac{\pi_\theta(y^i_j|x,y^{0:i}_j)}{\pi_{old}(y^i_j|x,y^{0:i}_j)},\alpha,\beta\right)}_{\text{token-level log-ratio clipping}}(r(x,y_j)-\bar r(x)) - \tau\log^2\!\left(\frac{\pi_\theta}{\pi_{old}}\right)\Bigg] $$
  • Token-level clipping: for any token whose log-ratio falls outside [α, β], the gradient is zeroed out. Unlike standard PPO, this ignores the sign of the advantage — it just clips off-policy drift.
  • That simple gradient masking turns out to be critical for long-horizon, multi-step tool-calling RL — it stops train-infer mismatch from amplifying.
  • Optimizer is still MuonClip (inherited from Kimi K2).

toggle — token-efficient RL

Designed to reconcile test-time scaling (longer thinking → stronger) with token budget (shorter reasoning → cheaper), attacking "length overfitting" head-on.

$$ \tilde r(x,y) = \begin{cases} r(x,y)\cdot \mathbb{I}\!\left\{\tfrac{1}{K}\sum_{i=1}^K r(x,y_i) < \lambda \ \text{ or }\ |y_i| \le \mathrm{budget}(x)\right\} & \lfloor t/m\rfloor \bmod 2 = 0 \ (\text{Phase 0}) \\[4pt] r(x,y) & \lfloor t/m\rfloor \bmod 2 = 1 \ (\text{Phase 1}) \end{cases} $$

Phase 0 · Budget-Limited

reward only if mean accuracy ≥ λ OR the response length is within budget.

budget(x) = Percentileρ({|yj| : r=1}) — estimated once before training starts and then fixed.

Phase 1 · Standard Scaling

open up to the full token limit and encourage test-time scaling.

the two phases alternate every m steps, giving a stochastic bi-objective optimization.

measured effect (Figure 5, on K2 Thinking)
Output tokens drop 25 – 30% with basically no quality loss; the redundant "verify-and-re-verify" loops shrink drastically. Even when trained only on math/coding, out-of-domain benchmarks like GPQA / MMLU-Pro also save tokens.

agent swarm & parl // from serial agent to parallel swarm

If the first half of the paper is about "sharpening the perception," this section is K2.5's other pillar — teaching the agent to fork itself. When I use K2-Thinking / Claude / Gemini for complex tasks, the pain has never been reasoning ability; it's the serialization — 50 searches queueing up one at a time, the 256K context getting eaten by tool-call outputs, wall-clock blowing past minutes.

K2.5's answer is clean when you look at it: build a trainable Orchestrator + a bunch of dynamically-instantiated, weight-frozen Sub-agents, and then use Parallel-Agent RL (PARL) to let the Orchestrator learn — on its own — when to fork, how many forks, what each fork does, and how to stitch the results back together. No hand-written scheduling rules anywhere; it's all discovered by RL. One word kept flashing through my head while I read this: emergence.

the three ceilings I kept hitting

latency grows linearly

T reasoning steps = T LLM forwards + T tool round-trips. Step count climbs, wall-clock explodes.

context blown out

tool history, search results, reasoning — all piled onto the same context. A long task chews through 256K fast.

reasoning-depth starvation

a single agent's "thinking budget" gets sliced by tool calls; real multi-branch verification gets squeezed out.

the architecture I pieced together — trainable orchestrator + frozen sub-agents

why freeze the sub-agents (the core of PARL)

  • dodges the two classic pitfalls of end-to-end joint optimization:
    — credit assignment ambiguity — a correct final answer doesn't mean every sub-agent was right, and vice-versa
    — training instability — outcome reward is sparse and noisy
  • treats sub-agent output as an environmental observation (not a differentiable decision node), cleanly splitting high-level coordination from low-level execution
  • sub-agents are instantiated from a fixed intermediate policy checkpoint
  • training accelerated: first train the Orchestrator against small-size sub-agents, then switch to the full-size model
  • the framework also adjusts the ratio of Orchestrator vs Sub-agent inference replicas dynamically to saturate the cluster

the decisions the Orchestrator owns

  • whether — should we parallelize at all (short tasks go straight through core tools)
  • when — at which step of the trajectory to spawn sub-tasks
  • how many — how many sub-agents to spawn at once
  • what role — what system_prompt to hand each sub-agent
  • how to aggregate — how to merge summaries coming back

All five are learned by RL from environment feedback. Prompts never explicitly say "go parallel."

my favorite piece: PARL reward = three terms + annealing

$$ r_{PARL}(x,y) = \underbrace{\lambda_1 \cdot r_{\text{parallel}}}_{\text{(1) instantiation reward}} \ + \ \underbrace{\lambda_2 \cdot r_{\text{finish}}}_{\text{(2) sub-task completion}} \ + \ \underbrace{r_{\text{perf}}(x, y)}_{\text{(3) task-level outcome}} $$
both auxiliary rewards use "bootstrap + anneal"
r_parallel and r_finish act like a warm-up — early in training they give the Orchestrator dense shaping reward to pull the policy out of "always serial"; then λ₁, λ₂ anneal smoothly to 0, handing optimization back to the real objective r_perf. It's this setup that lets PARL sidestep the classic multi-agent RL trap of collapsing under sparse reward.

critical steps — the ruler I finally understood

$$ \text{CriticalSteps} = \sum_{t=1}^{T}\left( S_{\text{main}}^{(t)} \ + \ \max_{i} S_{\text{sub},i}^{(t)} \right) $$
what Critical Steps means in practice
The paper puts it plainly: "the duration of stage t is governed by the longest-running sub-agent within that cohort". Using this quantity as the training & evaluation budget means:
  • spawning more forks without shortening the longest branch → zero gain (phony concurrency gets suppressed directly)
  • balanced decomposition + shorter longest branch → real payoff
  • forces the Orchestrator to learn "split by workload," not "more is more"

extending context at the architecture level

Swarm isn't compressing at the token level — it shards one long context across multiple independent sub-agents. Each sub-agent carries its own local working memory and only returns task-relevant, condensed output to the Orchestrator. The global context stays clean; modularity, information locality and reasoning integrity are all preserved at once.

Think of it as adding a new axis to context-length scaling: from token → architecture.

infrastructure // DEP + unified RL env

I've trained multimodal models myself, which is why this section hits hard — the real bottleneck in multimodal training isn't the model; it's the memory curve of PP Stage-0 twitching wildly every time a vision input comes in. The DEP (Decoupled Encoder Process) design K2.5 ships is one of the cleanest engineering fixes I've seen in the last six months, and I want to unpack why it's "clean" layer by layer.

decoupled encoder process (DEP)

the pit I stepped into tuning Kimi-VL
ViT and Text Embedding are pinned to PP Stage-0, but multimodal inputs have wildly varying image counts / resolutions → Stage-0's compute and memory look like an EKG. Like every other team I know, I hand-carved Stage-0's text layers to free up memory — bandaid fix, and there's no way to reuse the text-only parallel strategy we'd tuned to perfection.

unified agentic rl environment

framework design

  • Gym-like standard interface; pluggable Toolset · Judge · Prompt augmentation
  • each agent task is an independent async coroutine, can recursively launch sub-rollouts
  • Rollout Manager handles ~100K concurrent agent tasks
  • Partial Rollout support for better resource utilization

Token-in / Token-out protocol

  • strict logging of inference-engine log-probs
  • Train-Inference Mismatch Correction applied to keep RL stable
  • an LLM Gateway wraps black-box APIs into the same protocol
  • companion profiling / visualization / data-validation tooling

key code snippets

① sd2_tpool · the 4× temporal compression in 5 lines

def tpool_patch_merger(x, grid_thws, merge_kernel_size=(2, 2)):
    d_model = x.size(-1)
    outputs = []
    pre_sum = 0
    for t, h, w in grid_thws.tolist():
        seq = x[pre_sum:pre_sum + t * h * w]        # slice out the current sample
        kh, kw = merge_kernel_size
        new_h, new_w = h // kh, w // kw
        reshaped = seq.view(t, new_h, kh, new_w, kw, d_model)
        reshaped = reshaped.permute(0, 1, 3, 2, 4, 5).contiguous().mean(dim=0)
        #                                              ^^^^^^^^^^^^^
        #                                     ^^ this is the paper's "temporal pooling"
        outputs.append(reshaped.view(new_h * new_w, kh * kw, -1))
        pre_sum += t * h * w
    return outputs
paper ↔ code alignment
The paper says "consecutive frames are grouped in fours, processed through the shared MoonViT encoder, and temporally averaged at the patch level." In code, grid_thws's t is the number of frames in a group (≤4), and .mean(dim=0) does a patch-level average over all frames in the group — the genuine "shared weights + spatiotemporal volume."

② PatchMergerMLP · projection from vision to LLM

class PatchMergerMLP(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, config):
        super().__init__()
        # merge_kernel_size=(2,2) → self.hidden_size = 1152 * 4 = 4608
        self.hidden_size = config.mm_hidden_size * (
            config.merge_kernel_size[0] * config.merge_kernel_size[1])
        self.pre_norm = nn.LayerNorm(config.mm_hidden_size, eps=1e-5)
        self.proj = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(self.hidden_size, self.hidden_size),   # 4608 → 4608
            nn.GELU(),
            nn.Linear(self.hidden_size, config.hidden_size), # 4608 → 7168
        )

What _merge_input_ids_with_image_features does:

  1. build a _token_occupation_table: each <|media_pad|> placeholder occupies len(feature) positions
  2. use cumsum to compute each text token's new position new_token_positions in the merged sequence
  3. write text embeddings into final_embedding at the new positions first
  4. fill the remaining positions with vision features using the image_to_overwrite mask
  5. update attention_mask / position_ids / labels to match

2D position encoding — Learnable2DInterpPosEmbDivided_fixed

spatial 2D · learnable + bicubic interpolation

  • weight param: shape (64, 64, 1152)
  • pre-initialized on a 64×64 patch grid during training
  • for any (h, w): F.interpolate(bicubic) to (h, w, 1152)
  • wrapped by @torch.compile(dynamic=True) — first dynamic-shape call warms up
  • this is the mechanism that lets MoonViT support native resolution

temporal · fixed sincos

  • time_weight buffer: (4, 1, 1152) · non-persistent
  • generated by get_1d_sincos_pos_embed(1152, 4)
  • combine: pos_emb_3d[t] = pos_emb_2d + time_weight[0:t]
  • image is the t=1 degenerate case: zero extra temporal offset
  • video's t ∈ [1,4] matches init_pos_emb_time=4
$$ \text{pos\_emb}(t, h, w) = \underbrace{\text{bicubic}(\mathbf{W}_{\text{spatial}},\ \text{size}=(h,w))}_{\text{learnable — supports variable resolution}} + \underbrace{\text{sincos}(1152,\ 0{:}t)}_{\text{fixed — temporal}} $$

Rope2DPosEmbRepeated — 2D RoPE inside the ViT

# simplified formula (head_dim = 72, dim//4 = 18)
i ∈ [0, 18)
freqs[i]   = 1 / 10000^(4i/72)         # base=10000
x_freq[n]  = (n % max_width)  · freqs
y_freq[n]  = (n // max_width) · freqs

# complex rotation for every position
x_cis = cis(x_freq)     # shape (N, 18)
y_cis = cis(y_freq)     # shape (N, 18)

# interleave x and y rotations into a (max_h=512, max_w=512, 36) complex64 table
freqs_cis = interleave([x_cis, y_cis])
  • all 27 layers + 16 heads share a single freqs_cis table
  • for different (t, h, w), slice freqs_cis[:h, :w] and .repeat(t)
  • supports arbitrary image resolution, and reuses spatial position for the video temporal axis via repeat

flash_attn_varlen — the backbone of multi-sample packing

def multihead_attention(q, k, v, q_cu_seqlens, k_cu_seqlens, ...):
    attn_out = flash_attn_varlen_func(
        q, k, v,
        q_cu_seqlens, k_cu_seqlens,   # cumulative lengths
        max_seqlen_q, max_seqlen_k,
        causal=False,                  # ViT uses bidirectional (non-causal) attention
        deterministic=deterministic)
    return attn_out.flatten(start_dim=-2)
why it's called "Balanced Vision Forward"
A single forward can pack N images + M videos into one (L, 3, 14, 14) tensor, using cu_seqlens (cumulative lengths) to do block-diagonal attention — this is the foundation for DEP's "① Balanced Vision Forward" to load-balance across GPUs by patch count. The ViT is non-causal (causal=False), so no extra causal mask is needed on the block-diagonal.

quantization strategy, points that matter
  • only quantize MoE routed expert weights — this is where the bulk of the parameters live (384 experts × 2 × hidden × moe_inter each)
  • MLA self-attn, shared_expert, dense layer, lm_head all stay in BF16
  • only weights are quantized — activations and KV cache are NOT — inference precision is preserved
  • group_size=32 with symmetric + minmax plays nicely with vLLM / SGLang / KTransformers out of the box
  • SafeTensors param breakdown: F32=23,040 (scale/zero) · I32=1.01T (INT4 packed) · BF16=43.9B (non-expert)

from raw image to LLM — one-figure walkthrough

seven details this figure quietly nails down
  1. the vision encoder is ~400 M params (27 layers × ~15 M; includes PatchEmbed and 64×64×1152 pos-emb), matching the paper's "SigLIP-SO-400M init"
  2. ViT's head_dim is genuinely 72 — not 1152/16 misread as 64; 2D RoPE uses 36 complex pairs
  3. vision-token count varies with original image size — small images: dozens; big ones: thousands; max 4096 per image
  4. after sd2_tpool + PatchMerger, the final visual-token count entering the LLM = (h/2) × (w/2) per frame-chunk
  5. a 448×448 image → 32×32 patches → 16×16 = 256 × 7168-d tokens fed to K2
  6. same architecture handles images (t=1) and video (t=4), zero code branches
  7. native INT4 only touches MoE routed experts, so the vision tower stays BF16 end-to-end — no precision loss

takeaways // the 8 things I held onto after closing the paper

These are what stayed in my head after I closed the paper and shut the IDE. I sorted the 8 by how counter-intuitive they felt — each one I'd happily write another long piece about, but as a set of reading notes, I'll let the cards speak first.

[01] early fusion > late fusion

On a fixed vision+text budget, early fusion + flat low vision ratio (~10%) beats late high-ratio injection across the board. Breaks the "vision is a patch glued on after language" mental model.

[02] zero-vision sft

Text SFT data alone is enough to activate visual tool-calling and visual reasoning; adding hand-crafted visual SFT data actually hurts generalization.

[03] vision RL makes text stronger

MMLU-Pro +1.7, GPQA +2.1, LongBench v2 +2.2. Capabilities transfer both ways, no language regression.

[04] joint rl splits by ability, not modality

Knowledge / Reasoning / Coding / Agentic each consume both pure-text and multimodal; the GRM rates uniformly across modalities — maximizing cross-modal transfer.

[05] token-level log-ratio clipping

Ignores the sign of the advantage; clips gradients purely on off-policy drift. This is the quiet key to stable long-horizon tool-calling RL.

[06] toggle saves 25–30% tokens

Alternating Budget-Limited ↔ Standard-Scaling phases; a clean attack on length overfitting.

[07] context sharding > truncation

Agent Swarm shards a long context across sub-agents — active, structural context management beats passive truncation (BrowseComp 78.4 vs. Discard-all 74.9).

[08] engineering: DEP decouples the vision fwd/bwd

Decoupled Encoder Process gets multimodal training to 90% of pure-text throughput; K2.5 reuses K2's parallel strategy (PP16 × EP16 × ZeRO-1) as-is, no multimodal-specific tuning.

// END OF NOTEBOOK

I know the word count is getting away from me. What I actually think is this: Kimi K2.5 isn't "yet another slightly stronger VLM." It reworks multimodality, long context, tool use, and parallel agents at the same time, and at every seam it picks counter-intuitive but internally consistent options — Early Fusion, Zero-Vision SFT, ability-partitioned Joint RL, frozen-sub-agent PARL, DEP. Any one of these is paper-worthy on its own. My guess is the next generation of open VLMs will quietly standardise on at least three of them.
